Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is the dominant paradigm for training Large Language Model (LLM) agents on long-horizon tasks. However, sparse and delayed rewards often lead to tra-jectory neglect , in which agents lose focus on the task goal and interaction history at interme-diate steps. Prior work has explored step-level supervision using Shannon-entropy–based un-certainty signals, which conflate inherent state complexity with agent confidence and therefore provide unreliable estimates of decision relia-bility. To address this issue, we propose nor-malized entropy , which measures confidence deviations relative to an agent’s average behav-ior under a given state, thereby strengthening the association between low-quality actions and trajectory neglect. Building on this insight, we introduce Selective Trajectory-Aware Policy 

Optimization (STAPO), a hierarchical group-based RL framework. STAPO leverages nor-malized entropy to locate outlier steps associ-ated with trajectory neglect and optimizes them via a joint mechanism of trajectory-aware re-ward and trajectory-independent penalty , en-hancing trajectory awareness while preserving training stability. Extensive experiments on ALFWorld, WebShop, and Search-Augmented QA demonstrate that STAPO achieves state-of-the-art performance while substantially allevi-ating trajectory neglect, validating its effective-ness and robustness for agentic tasks.

While recent adaptations like GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025) and RLVMR (Zhang et al., 2025) at-tempt to mitigate the credit assignment issue by achieving fine-grained step-level advantage estima-tion, they typically optimize all steps indiscrimi-nately, lacking a selective mechanism to precisely locate and address these outlier steps caused by tra-jectory neglect. Furthermore, several works have explored leveraging internal uncertainty signals to assist training, including entropy-based regulariza-tion and supervision strategies (Xu et al., 2025; Cui et al., 2025). However, these approaches predom-inantly rely on Shannon entropy computed from step-level action distributions, which conflates in-herent state complexity with agent confidence. As shown in Figure 1, a high-entropy step may simply reflect a genuinely complex state with numerous plausible actions, rather than a lack of confidence. Consequently, raw Shannon entropy provides an unreliable signal for locating outlier steps associ-ated with trajectory neglect (as empirically illus-trated in Appendix B). To address these limitations, we first propose a novel metric: normalized entropy . By aggregating actions from the same state across sampled trajec-tories, normalized entropy measures uncertainty relative to the state average, effectively decoupling inherent state complexity from model confidence. Our empirical analysis shows that outliers located by normalized entropy consistently correlate with trajectory neglect, which we empirically find to be strongly associated with low-quality actions. Building on this insight, we introduce Selective 

Trajectory-Aware Policy Optimization (STAPO), a hierarchical group-based RL framework. STAPO operates in two phases: (1) Outlier Localization ,which dynamically locates outlier steps using nor-malized entropy statistics; and (2) Selective Op-timization , which targets these steps associated with trajectory neglect. Specifically, STAPO in-corporates a trajectory-aware reward to encour-age stronger focus on trajectories, balanced by a 

trajectory-independent penalty to mitigate the risk of reward hacking. Extensive experiments on representative agen-tic benchmarks, including ALFWorld (Shridhar et al., 2021), WebShop (Yao et al., 2022), and Search-Augmented QA, demonstrate that STAPO consistently outperforms strong baselines such as GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025) and EMPG (Wang et al., 2025a), while attribution analysis further confirms its effectiveness in alleviating trajectory neglect. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose normalized entropy to decouple state complexity from agent confidence and effectively locate low-quality actions and tra-jectory neglect. • We introduce STAPO , a hierarchical group-based RL framework that integrates out-lier localization with a joint optimization mechanism of trajectory-aware reward and trajectory-independent penalty. • Extensive experiments demonstrate that STAPO achieves superior performance and robustness across diverse agentic tasks, while substantially mitigating trajectory neglect. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 LLM Agents 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have increasingly been developed into autonomous agents capable of perceiving, reasoning, and acting in dynamic en-vironments (Zhang and Zhang, 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Zitkovich et al., 2023). Early approaches primarily relied on prompt-based methods, such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024), as well as enhanced memory, retrieval mech-anisms (Wang et al., 2024b; Tan et al., 2024), and integration with external tools (Schick et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). More recent work has shifted toward adapting model parameters through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Zhang and Zhang, 2024) or reinforcement learning (RL) (Sut-ton and Barto, 2018), enabling agents to learn di-rectly from environment interactions. 

2.2 Group-based Policy Optimization 

To avoid the high overhead and instability associ-ated with training independent critic models, group-based RL methods have emerged as promising al-ternatives to traditional actor-critic methods (like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017)). These algorithms, such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), RLOO (Kool et al., 2019; Ahmadian et al., 2024), and DAPO (Yu et al., 2025) leverage a group of samples from the same query and estimate the advantages ac-cordingly. Recently, GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025), RLVMR (Zhang et al., 2025), and Tree-GRPO (Ji et al., 2025) further advance this paradigm by intro-ducing hierarchical grouping structures to achieve fine-grained step-level advantage estimation with-out relying on auxiliary models. However, they optimize all steps indiscriminately, lacking a se-lective mechanism to precisely locate and address outlier steps caused by trajectory neglect. 

2.3 Entropy-based Reinforcement Learning 

To overcome the challenges of sparse external re-wards, recent works have explored using internal feedback (like model uncertainty, frequently quan-tified by entropy) to assist training (Agarwal et al., 2025). However, most of them rely on raw Shan-non entropy, which conflates inherent state com-plexity with agent confidence, and primarily utilize it as a proxy for regularization or re-weighting. For instance, EPO (Xu et al., 2025) stabilizes ex-ploration via entropy smoothing regularization; EMPG (Wang et al., 2025a) modulates the advan-tage term using entropy weights; and Cui et al. (2025) leverages entropy to truncate gradients for specific tokens. In contrast, we propose normal-ized entropy to decouple inherent state complexity from agent confidence, employing it to precisely locate outlier steps for selective trajectory-aware optimization.

4 Methodology 

To address the challenge of trajectory neglect iden-tified in Section 3, we propose Selective Trajectory-

Aware Policy Optimization (STAPO), a hierarchi-cal group-based RL framework. 

4.1 Outlier Localization Integration 

As illustrated in the left panel of Figure 3, STAPO integrates the localization mechanism validated in Section 3.2 directly into the training loop. Specif-ically, for each step, we calculate the normalized entropy Hn (Equation 2) and derive the indicator mask Mt (Equation 3). This mask functions as a dynamic gating mechanism: it filters the train-ing stream so that only steps flagged as outliers (Mt = 1 ) trigger the selective optimization mod-ule, while inliers ( Mt = 0 ) are optimized solely using the base objective.

5.5 Exploration Guidance 

STAPO demonstrates superior exploration capabil-ity, particularly in cold-start scenarios. In challeng-ing settings such as training Llama3.1-8B-Instruct on WebShop, the weak foundational policy fails to obtain any sparse outcome rewards, causing RL baselines like GiGPO to fail in optimizing effec-tively (as shown by the flatlined zero success rate in Figure 6). Conversely, STAPO’s trajectory-aware reward RT A provides continuous supervisory sig-nals. Especially in the initial phase (e.g., steps 0-50) where external outcome rewards are absent, 

RT A offers non-zero guidance, effectively steering the model towards meaningful actions and breaking the exploration deadlock. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we identify trajectory neglect as a pri-mary cause of low-quality actions in long-horizon tasks and propose normalized entropy to precisely locate it by decoupling state complexity from agent confidence. Building on this, we introduce STAPO, a framework that selectively optimizes outliers via a joint mechanism of trajectory-aware reward and trajectory-independent penalty. Extensive exper-iments demonstrate that STAPO achieves supe-rior performance and robustness across diverse benchmarks, providing a strong and competitive approach for trajectory-aware LLM agent training. 

Limitations 

Regarding training efficiency, calculating the trajectory-aware reward and trajectory-independent penalty requires constructing masked inputs and performing additional forward passes. However, as detailed in Appendix J, this computational over-head is marginal ( ∼5.7%) and proves significantly more cost-effective than scaling the group size. Our main experiments focus on text-based agents. Appendix L provides additional pilot re-sults on vision-language agents, but broader multi-modal settings and outlier behavior under diverse visual interfaces remain to be fully characterized. Normalized entropy assumes sufficient within-batch support for each anchor state: when a state is visited only once in a batch, intra-group normal-ization is ill-defined and the statistic effectively reduces to raw Shannon entropy, which again con-flates state complexity with confidence. Environ-ments with very large admissible action spaces make state revisitation rarer and can exacerbate this boundary behavior, occasionally yielding false positives or false negatives in outlier localization. 

Ethical considerations 

In this work, we introduce a framework to enhance the performance of LLM agents in long-horizon tasks. While improving agent capabilities is cru-cial for beneficial applications such as digital assis-tants and automated web navigation, we acknowl-edge that more capable agents could potentially be exploited for malicious purposes, such as auto-mated cyber-attacks or large-scale disinformation campaigns. However, our research is conducted strictly using established, public academic bench-marks (ALFWorld, WebShop, and QA datasets) that do not contain personally identifiable informa-tion (PII) or offensive content.

A Case Study of Trajectory Neglect in Multi-Turn Interactions 

To motivate the necessity of trajectory-aware op-timization, we analyze a failed trajectory of a Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct agent trained with GiGPO from ALFWorld. The task goal is “look at cd under the desklamp.” Throughout this episode, the agent exhibits two primary failure modes correlated with trajectory neglect: (1) Task Goal Neglect, where the agent wanders to irrelevant locations (e.g., the bed) despite the goal explicitly constraining the context to a desklamp, and (2) Interaction History Neglect, resulting in cyclic and redundant actions. 

Case: Neglect of Task Goal. As shown in Fig-ure 8, during Steps 21–25, the agent successfully acquires cd 1. However, instead of attempting to place it under a desklamp, the agent begins exam-ining the object and wanders to bed 1 (Step 23, highlighted with an underline), a location irrele-vant to the desklamp. This indicates a degradation of the goal signal over long horizons. 

Case: Neglect of Interaction History. As shown in Figure 9, during Steps 34–38, the agent demon-strates a severe inability to track its recent actions. After identifying cd 2 on shelf 1, the agent per-forms the action look at cd 2 . In the very next step, despite the observation remaining static, the agent repeats the exact same action (Step 35, high-lighted with an underline). Furthermore, the agent later returns to desk 2, a location it had already visited in Step 25. This suggests that the agent is not effectively tracking its interaction history. 

B Case Study: Normalized vs. Shannon Entropy 

To empirically validate the effectiveness of nor-malized entropy in decoupling state complexity from model confidence, we present qualitative ex-amples from the ALFWorld evaluation using the Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct base model. These cases demonstrate how normalized entropy avoids false positives in states with a large action space and reduces false negatives in states with a small action space, thereby providing a more reliable signal for outlier localization. 

Case: Avoiding False Positives in States with a Large Action Space. As shown in Figure 10, at Step 27, the agent is positioned in a kitchen en-vironment with a vast action space (32 selectable actions). The agent chooses to navigate to “coun-tertop 1”, a valid exploration step denoted by a pos-itive advantage. However, due to the sheer number of admissible actions, the raw probability distribu-tion is naturally dispersed, resulting in a high Shan-non entropy. A standard threshold-based method (e.g., IQR) would incorrectly flag this valid step as an outlier (a “false positive”). In contrast, normal-ized entropy calibrates this value against the group statistics of the same state. It correctly recognizes that high entropy is intrinsic to this specific state and classifies the step as an inlier, preventing the unnecessary penalization of valid exploration. 

Case: Reducing False Negatives in States with a Small Action Space. As shown in Figure 11, at Step 43, the agent operates in a restricted context with a limited action space (16 selectable actions). Here, the agent exhibits suboptimal behavior by closing an empty cabinet it has already inspected (interaction history neglect), a redundant action indicated by a negative advantage. Despite this low-quality decision, the limited number of choices results in a naturally sharper probability distribu-tion and a relatively low Shannon entropy. Conse-quently, a standard threshold-based method (e.g., IQR) would likely miss this error (a “false nega-tive”). However, normalized entropy detects that the agent’s raw Shannon entropy—while numeri-cally low—is significantly higher than the baseline expected for this specific state. This anomaly cor-rectly flags the step as an outlier, triggering the STAPO to optimize the behavior.

F Generalization and Adaptation 

To further verify the versatility and robustness of STAPO, we conduct extensive experiments focus-ing on its compatibility with advanced RL methods and its scalability across different model architec-tures. The results are summarized in Table 4 and Table 5. 

Generalization to Other Group-Based RL Meth-ods. The STAPO framework is orthogonal to ad-vancements in single-turn group-based RL, allow-ing it to seamlessly incorporate complementary techniques. To demonstrate this, we follow the setup of DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), which enhances GRPO with dynamic sampling and clip-higher mechanisms. We integrate these strategies into both GiGPO and STAPO, yielding variants denoted as GiGPO dynamic and STAPO dynamic . As shown in Table 4, while GiGPO dynamic improves over the DAPO baseline, STAPO dynamic achieves signifi-cantly superior results. Specifically, on WebShop, STAPO dynamic attains a success rate of 80.2%, out-performing GiGPO dynamic (75.0%) by a substantial margin. On ALFWorld, it reaches a remarkable 96.1% success rate. These results confirm that STAPO can effectively synergize with and amplify the benefits of DAPO, establishing a new state-of-the-art performance. 

Adaptation across Different Model Architec-tures and Scales. We further evaluate the scal-ability of STAPO by applying it to models with larger scales and different architectures: Llama3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct. As pre-sented in Table 5, STAPO consistently outper-forms the GiGPO baseline. For Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, STAPO improves the overall success rate on ALFWorld from 91.4% to 95.3%. Similarly, on the larger Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct model, STAPO pushes the performance to a near-perfect 97.2%, compared to 93.2% for GiGPO. This consistent improvement highlights that the trajectory-aware optimization in STAPO is model-agnostic and re-mains highly effective as model scale increases.

H Sensitivity Analysis of Weighting Coefficients α and γ

To investigate the impact of the selective optimiza-tion components on model performance, we con-duct a sensitivity analysis on the weighting coef-ficients for the trajectory-aware reward α and the trajectory-independent penalty γ.

Settings. We conduct this analysis on the Web-Shop benchmark using the Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct model. Based on the hypothesis that the reward sig-nal and the penalty constraint should be balanced to prevent optimization instability, we impose the constraint α = γ and sweep their values across the set {0, 0.005 , 0.01 , 0.02 , 0.04 }. The coefficient for the standard global KL penalty, β, is fixed at 0.01 throughout the experiments. Note that the setting 

α = γ = 0 corresponds to the GiGPO baseline, where no selective trajectory-aware optimization is applied. 

Analysis. The results, illustrated in Figure 7, demonstrate that STAPO benefits significantly from the proposed trajectory-aware mechanisms within a reasonable hyperparameter range. • Optimal Performance at Moderate Weights: The model achieves peak perfor-mance at α = γ = 0 .005 , attaining a reward score of 86.7 and a success rate of 73.9%. This represents a substantial improvement over the baseline, which yields a score of 83.1 and a success rate of 65.0%, demon-strating the effectiveness of introducing trajectory-aware supervision. • Robustness and Degradation: As the weights increase to 0.01 and 0.02, the per-formance decreases slightly from the peak but remains consistently superior to the baseline. However, further increasing the weights to 

α = γ = 0 .04 leads to a performance drop, with the score falling to 82.7, slightly below the baseline level (83.1). This suggests that excessively high weights cause the auxiliary objectives to dominate the total loss, overshad-owing the primary task reward and leading to optimization instability. In summary, STAPO demonstrates robustness to hyperparameter variations within the range of 

[0 .005 , 0.02] , with 0.005 providing the best results.
