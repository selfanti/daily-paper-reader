# Continuous-Time Reinforcement Learning for N -Player Stochastic Differential Games with Exploratory Policies 

Jisheng Liu 1 and Jing Zhang ∗11School of Mathematical Sciences, Fudan University, China 

Abstract 

We study continuous-time reinforcement learning for N -player noncooperative stochastic differential games. Each player adopts an entropy-regularized exploratory policy; given the others’ actions, the optimal response is a Gibbs distribution, and a Nash equilibrium requires these N conditional distributions to be jointly com-patible. We prove that the natural equilibrium concept—simultaneous Hamiltonian maximization—is equivalent to this compatibility, and establish a necessary and suf-ficient condition expressed as a computable cross-partial criterion on the optimal q-functions. Nash equilibria exist unconditionally for decoupled and symmetric games. When compatibility fails, a coordinate path integral construction yields an approxi-mate correlated equilibrium with explicit quadratic KL-divergence bounds that vanish locally uniformly as the exploration weight γ → ∞ . A q-function framework for the 

N -player game extends the single-agent q-learning theory of [21], with weak mar-tingale characterizations motivating model-free on-policy and off-policy algorithms. The framework extends to the ergodic (infinite-horizon) setting with the same locally uniform OR(1 /γ ) asymptotic rates. 

Keywords: stochastic differential games, reinforcement learning, exploratory policy, q-learning, Nash equilibrium, compatibility condition, entropy regularization, approximate correlated equilibrium

# 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning (RL) is a framework for sequential decision making under uncer-tainty, with applications in game playing and autonomous control; see, e.g., [34, 28]. While most of the RL literature focuses on discrete-time Markov decision processes (MDPs), re-cent work has developed rigorous foundations for RL in continuous time , where the state evolves according to a stochastic differential equation (SDE). Wang et al. introduced the exploratory formulation for continuous-time RL with con-tinuous state and action spaces in [36]: the agent samples actions from a stochastic policy

1arXiv:2607.19928v1 [math.OC] 22 Jul 2026 (a probability distribution over the action space), which amounts to using a relaxed con-trol. A Shannon entropy regularizer incorporated into the reward yields an exploratory HJB equation. The subsequent works [19, 20, 21] developed the supporting theory of policy evaluation, policy gradient, and q-learning in continuous time, all grounded in mar-tingale characterizations of value functions and q-functions. These foundations have been extended in several directions: regret analysis for exploratory policy improvement and 

q-learning [35], sublinear regret for continuous-time linear-quadratic (LQ) RL [17], data-driven exploration with adaptive entropy regularization [18], and continuous-time RL for optimal switching [16]. All of the above works treat single-agent stochastic control problems. However, many applications involve multiple decision makers whose objectives are coupled through the shared state process. N -player stochastic differential games (SDGs) provide the standard framework, with the Nash equilibrium (defined in [30])—each player’s strategy being opti-mal given the others’—as the classical solution concept. The theory is well developed; see, e.g., [1, 7, 6]. In the symmetric (homogeneous) case, the coupled HJB system collapses to a single equation and the equilibrium is characterized by a representative player’s fixed point, a reduction widely used in the mean-field limit N → ∞ (see in [5, 25, 15]). 

Asymmetric (heterogeneous) games—where players differ in dynamics, rewards, or constraints—present additional difficulties: the N coupled HJB equations remain distinct, no symmetry reduction is available, and existence/uniqueness of equilibria require delicate structural conditions, which is introduced in [3]. Explicit solutions are available only in special cases such as linear-quadratic games (see in [1] for example). Together with the curse of dimensionality, heterogeneity makes the asymmetric N -player problem difficult for analysis and computation; the recent α-potential game framework of [11] and its ap-plication to independent policy-gradient learning in LQ stochastic differential games [31] provide approximate equilibrium guarantees under structural conditions, but do not resolve the general case. Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) is a computational approach to multi-player games; see the surveys [4, 41, 14] and representative algorithms in [27]. How-ever, MARL algorithms are predominantly model-free and lack continuous-time theoretical grounding. On the continuous-time RL side, [10] studied entropy-regularized mean-field games and proved existence/uniqueness of the mean-field equilibrium, while [39] developed 

q-learning for mean-field (McKean–Vlasov) control. Both works focus on the N → ∞ limit and are cooperative in nature. Subsequent work extends continuous-time q-learning to MFG/MFC with common noise [40, 32] and to actor-critic algorithms for continuous-time MFG [26]. In the finite-N setting, entropy-regularized RL has been applied to two-player zero-sum stochastic differential games in [33], and recent work extends this to zero-sum and Stackelberg games with regime-switching jump-diffusions (see in [12, 13]). The rig-orous continuous-time RL theory for finite N -player general-sum noncooperative games— particularly asymmetric ones—has not been developed. 

Contributions of this paper. This paper develops a continuous-time exploratory RL framework for N -player noncooperative stochastic differential games, focusing on the asym-metric setting. Our main contributions are: 1. Exploratory formulation and conditional equilibrium. We extend the exploratory for-mulation of [36] to N -player games, where each player adopts a stochastic policy. 2The optimal policy for player i, conditioned on the actions of others, is a Gibbs distribution, and the question is compatibility : when do the individually optimal conditional policies of N players jointly form a Nash equilibrium? We introduce a natural equilibrium concept—simultaneously maximizing all Hamiltonians in the coupled HJB system—and prove its equivalence to the compatibility of conditional optimal policies (Theorem 12), so the conditional approach is without loss of gener-ality. Following [36], the running reward f i is assumed to depend on player i’s own action ui alone (Remark 4); the general case where f i depends on the full profile u

introduces additional coupling in the HJB system and is left for future work. 2. Compatibility analysis. We characterize the Nash equilibrium compatibility condi-tion, establishing its equivalence to (a) the existence of a compatible joint density, (b) a ϕ-factorization condition, and (c) an explicitly computable cross-partial crite-rion on the optimal q-functions. We further decompose the cross-partial into direct and indirect effects, identifying when compatibility holds, and prove unconditional equilibrium existence for decoupled and symmetric games. 3. Approximate correlated equilibrium. When compatibility fails, we construct an ap-proximate correlated equilibrium via a coordinate path integral method with explicit quadratic KL-divergence bounds. As γ → ∞ , the compatibility gap vanishes locally uniformly at rate OR(1 /γ ).4. q-Learning framework. We define a qi-function for each player and establish weak martingale characterizations (Theorems 34 and 37), which motivate model-free on-policy and off-policy algorithms. The compatibility condition is expressed directly in terms of the learned q-functions (Theorem 15), making it verifiable from algorithmic output. The framework extends to the ergodic (infinite-horizon) setting with the same asymptotic rates. The closest works are [36, 21] (single-agent continuous-time RL) and [10] (entropy-regularized mean-field games). We differ from [10] in considering finite N -player 

noncooperative games rather than mean-field limits, and from [39], whose q-learning for McKean–Vlasov control is cooperative. The unified q-learning framework of [40] covers both MFG and MFC but remains in the mean-field setting. The recent α-potential game framework [11, 31] addresses independent learning in LQ differential games from a policy-gradient perspective; by contrast, our compatibility charac-terization applies to general non-LQ games and is directly verifiable from learned 

q-functions. Among classical differential game theory, [1, 6] provide detailed treat-ments without the exploratory/learning perspective. The extension to jump-diffusion processes in the spirit of [8] introduces no essential difficulty and is left for future work. 

Organization. Section 2 introduces the necessary notation, while Section 3 formulates the exploratory problem and derives the associated HJB equations. Section 4 then exam-ines the compatibility condition, existence results, large-γ asymptotics, and approximate correlated equilibrium, with the ergodic extension following in Section 5. Turning to nu-merical aspects, Sections 6–7 develop the q-function theory and corresponding algorithms. 3Finally, numerical experiments are presented in Section 8, and Section 9 offers concluding remarks.

# 9 Concluding Remarks 

This paper develops a continuous-time RL framework for N -player stochastic differential games, covering formulation, equilibrium analysis, and algorithms. We introduced the natural equilibrium concept and proved its equivalence to the compatibility of conditional optimal policies (Theorem 12). Compatibility was charac-terized through three equivalent conditions (Theorem 13), expressed in terms of optimal 

q-functions (Theorem 15), and decomposed into direct and indirect effects (Proposition 17). Unconditional existence was established for decoupled (Theorem 18) and symmetric (The-orem 19) games, and the two-player case was characterized (Theorem 20). When com-patibility fails, we constructed an approximate correlated equilibrium with explicit KL-divergence bounds (Theorem 24). The framework extends to the infinite-horizon setting (Proposition 29, Corollary 30) with the same locally uniform OR(1 /γ ) rates. The q-learning theory of [21] was extended to the N -player game with weak martingale characterizations motivating model-free algorithms. Several directions remain open: the connection between our compatibility condition and the α-potential game framework of [11], which provides approximate equilibrium guaran-tees under different structural assumptions; applying the framework to structured games (Stackelberg, zero-sum, pursuer–evader) where sharper compatibility criteria may hold, extending the entropy-regularized RL approach of [33, 12]; the connection to mean-field 34 Figure 3: Ergodic equilibrium verification.
