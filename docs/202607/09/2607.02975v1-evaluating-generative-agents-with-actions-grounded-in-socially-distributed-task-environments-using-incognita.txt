## Abstract 

Effective agency in social environments depends on when an agent seeks knowledge, when it acts, and whether its actions are justified by the infor-mation it has acquired. Existing grounded benchmarks provide executable actions, persistent state, and verifiable outcomes, while social simulation en-vironments provide rich interaction among language agents. We study an evaluation setting that brings these requirements into a single environment. We define socially distributed task environments as interactive environ-ments in which task-relevant knowledge is partitioned across role-isolated participants, and consequential actions are reachable only through interac-tion with those participants. Under this view, communication functions as exploration over role-partitioned knowledge, and grounded action func-tions as exploitation over consequential environment state. We introduce Incognita, a Concordia-based environment framework that instantiates this setting. Incognita separates social interaction from grounded execution. The evaluated agent routes messages to a user or specialist entities; special-ist entities mediate admissible operations; a deterministic sub-environment executes accepted operations over a canonical state; and an offline evalu-ator scores final outcomes using inherited task rewards. Incognita-Retail transforms tau-bench retail into a multi-entity environment while preserv-ing its final-state reward semantics. We evaluate three generative agent models on 18 tasks stratified by social breadth, with 540 total trials. Model progress appears in both reward and behavior. Success rises from 0 percent to 8.9 percent and 17.2 percent, while premature finalization falls from 100 percent to 87 percent and 58 percent. Stronger models elicit more hidden knowledge, contact more entities, and attempt more grounded writes, yet reliability remains low. These findings suggest that socially distributed task environments expose agent behavior before reliable task success, including knowledge elicitation, source selection, grounded action attempts, and premature completion belief. 

## 1 Introduction 

Human action in organizations and service workflows is often conditioned by the social dis-tribution of knowledge, where no single participant holds all task-relevant information and effective action depends on identifying who can provide which part of it (SCH ¨UTZ, 1946). Formal accounts of distributed knowledge likewise treat communication as a mechanism that changes what agents can know individually and collectively (Halpern & Moses, 1990). As generative agents move from isolated instruction following to interactive environments, this structure becomes a central measurement problem for agent evaluation (Zhou et al., 2024a; Trivedi et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025). Evaluating such agents requires accounting for the final operation and the knowledge acquisition process that makes the operation appropriate (Yao et al., 2025; Laban et al., 2026; Qian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025).

Recent grounded benchmarks have made substantial progress by placing agents in web, app, tool, and customer-service environments with executable actions, persistent state, and verifiable outcomes (Zhou et al., 2024a; Trivedi et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025). In parallel, work on LLM-based social simulation and social intelligence has shown that language agents can sustain believable social behavior, open-ended social interaction, and multi-actor worlds mediated by natural language (Park et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024b; Vezhnevets et al., 2023). These two trajectories motivate an evaluation setting where social interaction is the mechanism for acquiring task-relevant knowledge and action remains grounded in consequential environment state (SCH ¨UTZ, 1946; Halpern & Moses, 1990; Yao et al., 2025; Vezhnevets et al., 2023). We study this setting through socially distributed task environments. We define a so-cially distributed task environment as an interactive environment in which task-relevant knowledge is partitioned across role-isolated participants, and consequential actions are reachable only through interaction with those participants. This formulation adapts the social distribution of knowledge to agent evaluation by placing knowledge distribution inside the environment design (SCH ¨UTZ, 1946). Borrowing the exploration-exploitation framing of Bayes-adaptive RL (Ross et al., 2007; Zintgraf et al., 2020), we cast communica-tion as exploration over role-partitioned knowledge and action as exploitation grounded in consequential environment state. This framing connects to evidence that LLMs degrade when task information is revealed across turns, including failures in clarification, question selection, context maintenance, and premature finalization (Laban et al., 2026; Qian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025), as well as methods proposed to mitigate such passivity (Wu et al., 2025). It also connects to benchmarks of information-asymmetric interaction, where agents must track who knows what, coordinate under unequal access to information, or interact with simulated coworkers in consequential tasks (Kim et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025), and to training methods that exploit asymmetric tool access (Eisenstein et al., 2025). The resulting evaluation problem is to measure how an agent allocates interaction across knowledge acquisition, source selection, and state-changing action within one grounded environment. We introduce Incognita, an environment framework built on Concordia for evaluating gen-erative agents with actions grounded in socially distributed task environments (Vezhnevets et al., 2023). Incognita separates the social interaction layer from the grounded execution layer. An evaluated agent has no direct tool access and can send one routed message per turn to a user or a specialist entity. The user holds demand-side task knowledge behind a disclosure gate, while specialist entities hold role-specific environment knowledge and mediate executable operations through scoped interfaces. Accepted operations are validated and executed by a deterministic sub-environment over a canonical state, and final outcomes are scored offline using the inherited task reward. Incognita-Retail instantiates this frame-work by transforming tau-bench retail into a multi-entity environment while preserving its final-state reward semantics (Yao et al., 2025). In a preliminary study, we evaluate three generative agent models on 18 Incognita-Retail tasks stratified by social breadth, with 10 repetitions per task and 540 total trials. The results show that model progress appears in both task reward and interaction behavior, as stronger models terminate less prematurely, elicit more hidden knowledge, contact more entities, and attempt more grounded actions. This paper makes three contributions. First, we formalize socially distributed task en-vironments as an evaluation setting for generative agents acting under role-partitioned knowledge. Second, we present Incognita as a Concordia-based environment framework that combines role-isolated interaction with deterministic state execution and offline re-ward evaluation. Third, we provide a preliminary empirical analysis showing how model progress changes exploration, exploitation, and failure modes in socially distributed task environments. 

## 2 Socially Distributed Environment Framework: Incognita 

Incognita is an environment framework for evaluating generative agents in socially dis-tributed task environments. It uses Concordia as the simulation substrate, following the 2Preprint. Under review.

Incognita specializes this substrate for evaluation. It exposes the test agent to role-isolated interaction, mediates consequential operations through specialist entities, and scores the final outcome outside the interaction loop. 

2.1 A test agent facing POMDP interface 

From the test agent’s perspective, an Incognita trial is a partially observable decision process. The agent does not observe the full task state. It receives socially mediated observations, chooses directed messages, and obtains reward only after the trial terminates. This motivates a compact POMDP description (Kaelbling et al., 1998). We write an Incognita trial as 

MI = ⟨S , A, T, Ω, Z, R⟩. (1) Here S denotes the latent environment state. It includes the canonical environment state, the task-relevant knowledge distributed across the USER and specialist entities, and the current interaction state. A denotes the test agent’s action space. Each action is a directed message at = ( rt, mt, ft), where rt is the selected recipient, mt is the message content, and 

ft ∈ { 0, 1 } is a finalization flag. Ω denotes the observation space. Observations may come from the USER, from specialist entities, or from narrated results of grounded operations. These sources enter the test agent through a single directed interaction interface. T describes message routing, participant responses, entity-mediated operations, and updates to the canonical environment state. Z(ot+1 | st+1, at) describes how a latent state and an action produce the next socially mediated observation. R denotes the terminal reward computed by the offline evaluator. 3Preprint. Under review. The test agent observes the history ht = ( o0, a0, o1, . . . , at−1, ot) and forms an implicit belief state bt(s) = P(st = s | ht).We use belief state as an analytic abstraction. It is not a claim that a language model explicitly computes a calibrated probability distribution. The abstraction captures the central evaluation problem. The agent must update its internal estimate of user intent, role-specific knowledge, feasible operations, and task completion through interaction. This view makes three design choices explicit. Communication updates the agent’s belief state. Entity-mediated operations ground that belief in environment state. Finalization commits the agent to the claim that the user goal has been satisfied. 

2.2 Redistributing task-relevant knowledge 

Incognita changes the informational topology of a source benchmark. A task that was originally presented through a centralized user interaction, policy context, and tool interface is converted into a socially distributed environment. The task specification becomes a task card. Its content is selectively rendered into the environment according to role and timing. The test agent receives neither the full task-relevant knowledge nor direct access to grounded operations at initialization. This transformation has two axes. The user side contains demand-side knowledge. It is exposed through the USER and controlled by a disclosure mechanism. The environment side contains role-specific knowledge about what can be inspected or changed. It is exposed through specialist entities. In the fully socially distributed condition, both axes are hidden from the test agent at the beginning of the trial. The design places task-relevant knowledge inside the environment state. The agent’s prob-lem is therefore not limited to choosing the next operation. It must infer which knowledge exists, who holds it, and whether the current belief state is sufficient for consequential action. In this sense, Incognita makes the informational structure of the environment part of the evaluation object. 

2.3 Communication as belief state update 

Communication is the agent’s observation-generating action. At each turn, the test agent selects one participant and sends a message. The chosen participant returns an observation according to its local role and local knowledge. The USER can answer only from the knowledge that has been disclosed. Each specialist entity responds within its assigned remit and does not observe the USER dialogue or other entity exchanges. This directed interaction implements partial observability at the social layer. The test agent is the only role with a global interaction history. Other participants maintain local views. As a result, every message is also an information-seeking action. A question to the USER may reveal user-side task knowledge. A question to an entity may reveal role-specific knowledge, required arguments, or a grounded read result. The observation updates the agent’s belief state over the latent task state. We denote this update abstractly as 

bt+1 = U(bt, at, ot+1). (2) The update function U is not implemented as an explicit Bayesian filter. It represents the behavioral requirement imposed on the language agent by the environment. The agent must integrate observations over time and use them to guide later communication, action, and finalization. 

2.4 Grounded action as state changing exploitation 

A grounded action occurs when the agent converts its belief state into an operation that the environment accepts and realizes. The test agent has no direct tool access. To affect 4Preprint. Under review. the environment, it must communicate with a specialist entity that can mediate the corre-sponding operation. If the entity proposes an operation, the proposal enters the grounded sub-environment. The sub-environment has two responsibilities. It validates whether the operation belongs to the proposing entity and whether the supplied arguments are admissible. It then executes accepted operations against the canonical environment state. Reads return information while preserving state. Writes mutate state and create the consequences that will later be evaluated. We can express the grounded component of the transition as 

xt+1 = Tx (xt, at, ot+1), (3) where xt is the canonical environment state. Only accepted write operations change xt.This distinction matters because exploration and exploitation are both present in the same interaction process. Some actions produce observations that refine the belief state. Other actions commit the current belief to a state changing operation. This is the sense in which Incognita grounds agent action. The agent’s language output does not directly count as success. It must pass through role mediation, validation, execution, and final state evaluation. 

2.5 Finalization as task completion belief 

The finalization flag is a core part of the agent interface. When the test agent sends a message with ft = 1, the trial terminates. This action is treated as an observable commitment of the agent’s task-completion belief. Let G(s) ∈ { 0, 1 } denote whether the source task has been completed under the evaluator. The test agent does not observe G(st) directly during interaction. It can only estimate whether the user goal has been satisfied from its belief state. We write this task-completion belief as 

qt = Es∼bt [G(s)] . (4) The environment does not require access to the agent’s internal threshold. The final action is sufficient as behavioral evidence. By finalizing, the agent declares that its current belief state supports termination. If the offline reward later fails, especially before the necessary grounded operations have occurred, the final action reveals a mismatch between the agent’s task-completion belief and the canonical environment state. This design makes premature finalization a meaningful measurement signal. It is not only a runtime stopping condition. It indicates that the agent judged the task as complete under insufficient distributed knowledge or insufficient grounded action. 

2.6 Offline evaluation as verification of commitment 

Incognita keeps evaluation outside the interaction loop. The evaluator does not guide the agent, update participant behavior, or inspect the agent’s belief state. After termination, it reads the final environment state and the task card, then applies the inherited task reward. In Incognita-Retail, this reward follows the tau-bench evaluation structure (Yao et al., 2025). The database channel compares the final state with a replayed gold state. Natural language assertions are checked only when the source task specifies them. A trial succeeds when all operative reward channels pass. This separation preserves the distinction between belief formation, grounded action, and outcome verification. Communication produces observations. Entity-mediated operations produce state consequences. Finalization exposes the agent’s completion belief. The offline evaluator determines whether the resulting environment state satisfies the source task. ## 3 Preliminary Results 

We evaluate whether the Incognita interface makes the exploration, exploitation, and final-ization signals from Section 2 visible at scale. The study varies only the evaluated agent across gpt-5.2, gpt-5.4, and gpt-5.5. The USER, specialist entities, and disclosure gate are fixed. The corpus contains 18 Incognita-Retail tasks stratified by social breadth from one to six, with 10 repetitions per task and 180 trials per model. Social breadth is the number of distinct specialist entities that the reference solution must engage. The primary outcome is the inherited tau reward, which requires all operative reward channels to pass. Figure 2 shows that grounded task success remains sparse. gpt-5.2 obtains no reward. gpt-5.4 succeeds only at breadth 3 and 4, reaching 8.9 percent reward overall. gpt-5.5 reaches 17.2 percent reward overall and obtains nonzero reward at every breadth level, although breadth 6 remains nearly unsolved. We use social breadth as a stratification of required social engagement. Task difficulty remains multidimensional. This pattern supports the measurement premise of Incognita. Increasing model strength appears as broader but still fragile conversion of socially acquired knowledge into verified state change. The environment therefore preserves grounded outcome evaluation while exposing how success depends on interaction under role-partitioned knowledge. Figure 3a shows that model improvement changes failure composition. gpt-5.2 finalizes prematurely in every trial. gpt-5.4 reduces this pattern to 87 percent and reaches 8.9 percent reward. gpt-5.5 reduces premature finalization to 58 percent and reaches 17.2 percent reward. The remaining failures increasingly involve environment-belief, goal-belief, and execution errors. These categories indicate that stronger agents spend more trials inside the intended inter-action regime. They no longer fail only by immediate termination. They also fail after nontrivial belief formation, entity interaction, and grounded action attempts. This is the behavior space that the socially distributed environment was designed to make measurable. Figure 3b explains this shift. Disclosure coverage rises from 0.06 to 0.17 to 0.45. Write rate rises from 0 to 0.13 to 0.36. Non-premature rate rises from 0 to 0.13 to 0.42. Entity-contact breadth rises from 0.30 to 0.68 by gpt-5.4 and remains stable for gpt-5.5. The later gain is concentrated in user-side disclosure, grounded writes, and finalization timing. Together, the results show a behavioral progression from premature completion belief to-ward sustained exploration and grounded exploitation. The reward remains low, which indicates unresolved reliability. The process metrics show where model progress occurs before reliable task completion. ## 4 Conclusion 

Human action under socially distributed knowledge depends on identifying relevant sources, integrating partial observations, and committing to action only when the acquired knowledge supports that commitment. Incognita translates this structure into an evaluation framework for generative agents by coupling role-isolated interaction with deterministic state execution and offline reward verification. The preliminary Incognita-Retail study shows that model progress is not captured by final reward alone. Stronger agents move from premature completion belief toward sustained knowledge seeking, broader source selection, and more frequent grounded action attempts, while their remaining failures ex-pose unresolved gaps in belief formation, action grounding, and finalization. These findings support socially distributed task environments as a productive measurement setting for agent behavior. They make communication, state-changing action, and task-completion commitment observable within a single grounded environment, providing a path for evalu-ating collaborative agency without reducing it to open-ended dialogue quality or final task success alone.

## B Use of LLMs 

This work uses large language models as substantive components of the research. In the Incognita-Retail experiments, LLMs instantiate the evaluated generative agents, the USER simulator, the specialist-entity simulators, and the disclosure gate. The evaluated agent 12 Preprint. Under review. models are gpt-5.2-2025-12-11, gpt-5.4-2026-03-05, and gpt-5.5-2026-04-23, each run with high reasoning effort and temperature 1. The USER, six specialist entities, and disclosure gate are fixed to gpt-5.4-2026-03-05 with temperature 0. For tasks with natural-language assertions, an LLM-based judge, gpt-4.1-2025-04-14 at temperature 0, is used as part of the inherited tau-bench evaluation procedure. Thus, LLMs are used not only for text generation but also as experimental agents, environment participants, a disclosure mechanism, and, where applicable, evaluation components. LLMs were also used during the preparation of this manuscript to polish and refine author-drafted text, assist with generating and checking implementation code, and help check the consistency of reported experimental results against the underlying logs, tables, and analysis outputs. The research ideas, experimental design, environment design decisions, and analytical conclusions were produced by the authors. All LLM-generated or LLM-assisted content was reviewed, verified, and revised by the authors, who take full responsibility for the accuracy, originality, and integrity of this work. 13
