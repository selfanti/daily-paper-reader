## ABSTRACT 

A self-evolving agentic loop repeatedly proposes a tweaked version of an agent (its prompt template or program) and accepts or rejects the change based on a per-iteration quality signal. Designing that signal is often the costly part of the project: a reliable scalar reward requires domain expertise and labeled examples that are themselves as expensive to assemble as the agent’s underlying task. We propose replacing the scalar at the accept/reject gate with a pairwise validator : a frozen LLM that, given the parent and child candidate, returns a binary verdict on which is better. Pairwise judgment is generally easier and more stable than absolute scoring, due to its contrastive nature, which mitigates the need for strict scale calibration. The validator also requires no training of its own. We integrate the validator into three published self-evolving engines (GEPA, ADRS, ShinkaEvolve) and report two flavors: Adaptive Focus , which retains the engine’s existing val-set parent selection, and Soft Elo , which lets the validator’s verdicts drive parent selection so that val-set rewards drop as well. Across multiple agents and two artifact substrates (prompt and code), our method matches or exceeds the full-reward baseline on the majority of settings we evaluate, and the pattern survives a cross-family validator swap. The pairwise gate is thus a drop-in replacement for per-step reward design at competitive task accuracy without the labeling cost. 

## 1 Introduction 

Modern AI agents — LLMs that solve multi-step tasks by combining a prompt, tool calls, and control logic — are increasingly built and refined automatically rather than hand-tuned. One growing class of systems improves an agent 

without retraining the underlying model : each iteration, the agent self-evolves by modifying the prompt template, the program, or the agentic workflow graph: a single piece of the agent’s surrounding harness or scaffolding . The agent then runs this new version against a quality signal, and keeps the modification if the signal improves. Because the underlying model parameters remain frozen, such a self-evolution can be applied whether the model is open-weight or accessed only through an API. We refer to this class of systems as self-evolving agentic loops . Existing literature along this line include GEPA [1] for prompt evolution, ADRS [4] and ShinkaEvolve [15] for code evolution, and AlphaEvolve [12] and AFlow [27] for related substrates. What unifies them is that the evolution progresses by referencing to only a scalar quality signal, namely a reward, which drives the per-iteration evolution direction and mutation selection. Building that scalar signal or reward for evolution is often considered expensive. Reward design takes domain expertise and frequently requires clean, labeled examples that are themselves costly to assemble — for many real tasks, labeling a single example can cost more than running the agent itself. In some scenarios, such reward is hard to define or impossible to collect. The result is a practical asymmetry: collecting a few examples of what good and bad outputs look like is often easy, but collecting enough clean examples to define a reliable per-step reward is not. Meanwhile, recent work on RLAIF [2] and LLM-as-judge [30] has shown that a frozen LLM, prompted with a handful of pairwise examples, can substitute for human preference labels in policy training. We ask the natural follow-up question: can a few-shot LLM validator substitute for the per-step task reward inside a self-evolving agentic loop’s accept/reject decision? Our answer is a pairwise validator at the per-iteration accept/reject decision. ## 2 Related Work 

Pairwise feedback and LLM-as-judge . Pairwise feedback can substitute for scalar rewards in two settings. In policy training, pairwise feedback replaces explicit scalar rewards: RLHF [6] learns a reward model from human pairwise preferences, RLAIF and Constitutional AI [2] substitute LLM-generated preferences for human ones with comparable downstream quality, and LLM-as-judge methods [30] together with Chatbot Arena [5] show that frozen LLMs achieve high agreement with humans on pairwise comparisons across diverse tasks — the property that makes them usable at the gate without an additional preference-learning step. In evolutionary search, non-scalar selection avoids reducing to a single fitness number: Quality-Diversity through AI / Human Feedback [3, 8] uses LLM- or human-derived similarity to maintain diversity niches, preference-based evolutionary multi-objective optimization [13] uses dueling bandits in lieu of scalar fitness, and multi-objective optimization itself [7, 28] maintains a Pareto front over competing objectives. Both lines apply the preference signal over a pre-collected population. Self-evolving agent frameworks . Self-evolving agent frameworks share a common loop: propose a candidate succes-sor of an artifact (prompt, program, workflow), accept or reject the candidate against a quality signal, then select the next parent. GEPA [1] optimizes prompts through reflective LLM-driven mutation with parent selection on a few-shot validation set. ADRS [4] and ShinkaEvolve [15] optimize code, the former through a parallel program database, the latter through reflective sampling with novelty pressure. AlphaEvolve [12] and OpenEvolve [19] apply analogous loops at larger scale. AFlow [27] evolves agentic workflow graphs. The Darwin Gödel Machine [26] self-improves an entire agent codebase. Recent surveys consolidate the landscape [11, 9]. Every published variant requires a scalar reward to drive selection. We replace that scalar reward with a pairwise validator at the accept/reject step. 

Pairwise judges inside self-evolving agents . The closest precedents combine pairwise judgment with self-evolving agents but stop short of substituting the per-step reward. CodeFavor [17] learns code preference models from synthetic evolution data but uses them outside the evolution loop. ExPairT-LLM [25] pairwise-judges code candidates in a static benchmark setting. ScoreFlow [22] uses pairwise preference inside agentic workflow optimization but on task performance, not as a reward substitute. MADE [29] explores reward-free evolution through a different mechanism. Feedback Descent [16] applies LLM textual feedback as a gradient-like signal outside the evolution loop, not at the gate. No prior system places a pairwise validator at the per-iteration accept/reject step as a replacement for the per-step task reward. That is the gap we address. 

## 3 Preliminaries 

Self-evolving agentic loops . Each self-evolving agent refines a target component, such as a prompt or a piece of code, by iteratively proposing, accepting, and selecting candidates. The iteration generally follows three steps: (1) Propose. 

Given a current “parent” candidate, the mutator generates a new “child” candidate by applying a modification. (2) Ac-cept or reject. A gate decides whether to keep the child by comparing parent and child against the engine’s per-step task reward. (3) Select. A selector picks the next parent from the pool for the next iteration. This paper redesigns the gate (step (2) ) and, in our Soft Elo configuration, the selector (step (3) ); the propose step follows each engine’s default. Data pools. We partition each benchmark’s data into three disjoint pools. The validation pool V, with |V| ∈ [12 , 30] 

examples per benchmark, is deliberately smaller than the validation sets used in typical self-evolving setups — we choose this low-data regime because labeling more examples is expensive. The full-reward baseline and our method use the same V. The train pool T feeds the engine’s mutator. In our method, the validator gate also draws a per-iteration minibatch Tt ⊆ T from it. The remaining examples form a held-out test set used only for final reporting in §5.

4.2 Adaptive Focus (training-reward-free) 

Adaptive Focus extends the validator gate by making the context E a periodically-refreshed rubric handed to Vϕ at gate time; the rubric is the only thing this configuration changes. Parent selection (step (3) ) follows the engine’s default rule over validation rewards on V.

Three rubrics. The rubric is one of three pre-defined options, each broader than the previous: 1. Accuracy : ask which response has the better final answer. 2. Soundness : ask which response gives the better answer with a clear reasoning chain. 3. Holistic : ask which response is stronger overall, weighing correctness, reasoning, completeness, clarity, and formatting together. At gate time, the validator selects one of these three rubrics as context E, directing focus to a different dimension of the pairwise decision. 

Periodic rubric refresh. The active rubric is refreshed every 10 iterations by a meta-LLM call. The meta-LLM is shown a compact window of recent gate activity — gate decisions over the last 10 iterations and a small set of recent (parent, child) output snippets — and outputs one of the three rubrics for the next window. The rubric is initialized at 

Accuracy ; there is no fixed schedule, though we empirically observe that the rubric tends to shift toward Holistic over the course of a run. 

4.3 Soft Elo (Fully Reward-Free) 

Soft Elo extends the validator gate from Eq. (3) with E = ∅, and replaces the engine’s default parent-selection rule (step (3)) with an Elo rating system [10, 5] over pairwise verdicts. Soft Elo is therefore completely reward-free during evolution. Each candidate in the engine’s candidate pool carries a rating R, initialized at 1600 . ## 5 Results 

Replacing the per-step reward with a pairwise validator does not cost us on task accuracy: across multiple agents and engines, our method matches or exceeds the full-reward baseline on the majority of cells we measure (§5.2), and on cells where validation saturates early it shows a smaller validation-to-test gap than the baseline. The pattern survives a cross-family validator swap (§5.3) and reproduces on cross-substrate cells under code evolution (§5.4). Reaching the baseline is the result: we removed the per-step reward and the loop’s task score survived.

Why a different variant slate. The variant slate in Table 5 differs from the prompt-evolution slate of §5.2 for an engine-side reason: ADRS and ShinkaEvolve do not maintain a held-out validation pool V — parent selection is driven by the engine’s full task evaluator, which is itself the costly object Vϕ is meant to replace. ## 6 Conclusion 

Designing the per-step task reward is often the most expensive part of a self-evolving agent building loop — it depends on labeled data and domain expertise that are themselves comparable in cost to the agent’s underlying task. Replacing that scalar with a pairwise validator that returns a binary preference between parent and child preserves the loop’s 8Reward-Free Evolving Agents via Pairwise Validator A P REPRINT 

ability to make progress: across prompt evolution (five agent configurations under GEPA) and code evolution (under two engines, ADRS and ShinkaEvolve), our pairwise gate matches or exceeds the full-reward baseline on the majority of cells we evaluate, and the pattern survives a cross-family validator swap. The substitution works best when the baseline still has room to improve and the validator can reliably tell parent from child; where the validator itself struggles on the task type (the Gemma-4 PUPA case), pairwise verdicts become noisy and the gate stops adding value. 

Limitations. The gain is sensitive to four regimes that bound where the substitution holds: 1) saturation, where the baseline is already near the agent’s ceiling and there is no headroom for the gate to exploit; 2) small-N test sets, where a single problem moves the score and per-cell deltas read as noise; 3) agent-capability gaps, where the agent fundamentally lacks the ability to make progress on a task and a validator gate cannot surface ability that is not there; 4) validator-capability mismatch, where the validator itself struggles on the task type and pairwise verdicts become unreliable. Each regime points to a concrete next experiment. 

Open follow-ups. Three directions extend the picture beyond what we report here. Broader cross-substrate coverage — more code engines and more task families — would test the substitution where parent selection is itself the costly object. A deeper study of how validator capability interacts with agent capability under reward-free evolution would resolve what our two cross-family swaps only sketch. And a head-to-head against a multi-objective Pareto formulation, which treats the validator score as a coordinate alongside the task reward rather than as a binary gate, would settle the gate-vs-objective question. The reading is one of scope, not strength: a few-shot pairwise validator does not replace a per-step reward universally, but on cells where the per-step reward is the costly object, the substitution holds up.

## D Engine Integration 

The validator gate (Eq. (3), §4.1) is implemented as a single accept_or_reject(parent, child) callback — with 

parent and child corresponding to ft,p and ft,c from §3 — that lands after a child has been generated and any task-side scoring has completed, but before the engine commits the child to the population. Insertion is a few lines per engine and is fully reversible: • GEPA : after the subsample acceptance check, before the full validation evaluation [1]. • ADRS : before the candidate is added to the program database [4]. • ShinkaEvolve : at the equivalent acceptance point in the parallel sampler [15]. Hyperparameters (population size, mutation operators, sampler temperatures) stay at each engine’s defaults. The only knobs we vary are the accept/reject rule and (for Soft Elo, §4.3) the parent-selection rule; this separation isolates the contribution of the gate from the contribution of the parent-selection algorithm.
