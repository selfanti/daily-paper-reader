# Agent Explorative Policy Optimization for Multimodal Agentic Reasoning

Abstract 

Vision-language models with extended reasoning succeed on complex problems, but many real-world problems require external tools that internal reasoning alone often cannot resolve. Agentic reasoning therefore interleaves two behaviors with a structural asymmetry: thinking (the self-contained default) and tool use (a high-variance auxiliary acting). We refer to this asymmetry as the Thinking-Acting Gap . Under standard RL recipes like GRPO, the gap manifests as two diagnostic symptoms during training: tool use is attempted on only ∼30% 

of rollouts, and when attempted, the tool-using rollouts within a group are all-wrong on ∼40% of questions, suppressing the learning signal at the tool calls that needed it. We propose AXPO (Agent e Xplorative 

Policy Optimization): for each all-wrong tool-using subgroup, AXPO fixes the thinking prefix and resamples the tool call and its continuation, paired with uncertainty-based prefix selection. Across nine multimodal benchmarks and three scales of Qwen3-VL-Thinking, SFT + AXPO outperforms SFT + GRPO at average (+1 .8 pp Pass@1 and +1 .8 pp Pass@4 at 8B on average) and 8B with SFT + AXPO surpasses the 32B Base on Pass@4 with 4× fewer parameters. ## 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) with extended reasoning capabilities have made remarkable progress, producing chains of thought that rival much larger models on mathematical and scientific reasoning benchmarks [ 1 –3 ]. These reasoning models invest test-time compute into internal search and reflection to arrive at accurate answers. However, many real-world problems lie beyond what internal reasoning alone can resolve: retrieving up-to-date knowledge, performing complex numerical computation, and understanding fine-grained visual details all require access to external resources [ 4 –8]. Tools such as web search, code interpreters, and image manipulation utilities can fill this gap, enabling what we call Multimodal Agentic Reasoning :the model autonomously decides when to think further and when to act through tools, much as it

and Tool use (Acting) . Thinking is the default mode, self-contained within the model’s token space: every token is under its control and errors can be corrected by exploring a different chain of thought [ 15 – 17 ]. Tool use , by contrast, is an auxiliary behavior that is simultaneously powerful but fragile: a short token sequence triggers an external response the model cannot predict or control, and small changes in the call can produce drastically different outcomes (e.g., invalid python code with wrong indent) [ 18 ]. Reasoning models are heavily post-trained for thinking, but far less for acting. We refer to this asymmetry between a safe default and a high-variance auxiliary as the 

Thinking-Acting Gap .Due to this gap, eliciting agentic reasoning remains a challenge despite the capabilities of recent reasoning VLMs [ 19 ]. Relying solely on prompting is often insufficient to ensure an appropriate tool call when needed. This necessitates post-training pipelines, such as Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL), as evidenced in Figure 1. SFT reliably teaches the 

mechanics of tool use but struggles to teach the model to utilize tools better for situations the teacher’s demonstrations did not cover. The resulting agent inherits the teacher’s failure modes and often plateaus near its performance [ 20 , 21 ]. Therefore, the model requires a training signal that goes beyond imitation, which motivates utilizing RL with verifiable outcome rewards after SFT [18, 22, 23]. However, RL methods such as GRPO [ 24 – 26 ] are suboptimal to make models proficient at acting, because two patterns suppress the tool-call learning signal. First, tool use remains a minority of rollouts compared with thinking-only (Figure 3a). Second, when tool use is attempted, the entire group of rollouts is far more likely to fail together (Figure 3b). Under GRPO, this asymmetry weakens the tool-call learning signal in both regimes: tool-using rollouts trail thinking-only successes in mixed groups (negative advantage) and zero out in all-wrong groups [ 27 , 28 ]. These observations motivate targeted exploration for tool use during RL. This raises a critical question: how can we effectively induce such exploration? The Thinking-Acting Gap suggests the answer. Thinking is the native mode of the model and the tool call is the separate mode that turns an intent into a concrete action where the same intent admits diverse implementations (Figure 3c). Building on this observation, our key insight is that resampling at the tool call lets the model explore diverse agentic reasoning: we anchor the thinking prefix and resample the tool call and its continuation (Figure 2). We call this tool-call resampling . The operation has no analog in pure reasoning, where there is no comparable boundary between a committed intent and a high-variance

action. When a tool-using trajectory fails, the thinking prefix may be entirely sound. Re-deriving the full trajectory from scratch wastes budget on prefixes that were not the bottleneck, whereas tool-call resampling concentrates exploration precisely where the Thinking-Acting Gap manifests. We propose AXPO (Agent e Xplorative Policy Optimization) based on this principle to narrow the gap during RL. Concretely, AXPO instantiates three design choices on top of a small extra rollout budget: (1) 

where to resample , only on all-wrong tool-using subgroups where a single recovery flips the GRPO advantage; (2) which prefix , the highest-uncertainty ones; and (3) how resamples enter the gradient ,a per-prefix GRPO advantage on continuations and a binary recovery indicator on the source prefix. Unlike prior reward-shaping methods [ 18 , 23 , 29 ] or rollout scaling methods [ 30 , 31 ], AXPO restructures the rollout distribution itself at the locus of the Thinking-Acting Gap. Across nine multimodal benchmarks at Qwen3-VL-Thinking 2B/4B/8B [ 19 ], SFT + AXPO outper-forms SFT + GRPO at average with Pass@4 gains of +2 .8/+2 .3/+1 .8 pp. At 8B, SFT + AXPO surpasses the 32B Base on Pass@4 and reaches 99% of its Pass@1, despite using 4× fewer param-eters (Figure 1). The improvement concentrates on tool-intensive benchmarks, while preserving the performance improvement in reasoning benchmarks compared to SFT + GRPO baseline. Notably, 

+25% extra resampling budget with AXPO delivers +1 .1 pp over +100% extra rollout budget with GRPO, confirming that the gain comes from where compute is spent. Our contributions are as follows: 

• We identify the Thinking-Acting Gap , an asymmetry between thinking and tool use that manifests in group-based agentic RL as two symptoms: tool use is attempted on 30% of rollouts and tool-using subgroups (the tool-using rollouts within a group) are all-wrong on 40% of questions, yielding non-positive advantage on tool-call tokens. 

• We propose AXPO , an RL algorithm that closes the gap via tool-call resampling : for all-wrong tool-using subgroups, AXPO fixes the thinking prefix at uncertain tool calls and resamples from them, provably dominating from-scratch sampling on tool-use coverage at fixed budget. 

• We validate AXPO on nine multimodal benchmarks across three scales of Qwen3-VL-Thinking, with gains for all sizes compared to GRPO baseline, and the resulting 2B model matches the 8B Base and the 8B model surpasses the 32B Base on Pass@4 .

2.2. Diagnosing the Thinking-Acting Gap 

We now ground the Thinking-Acting Gap in three diagnostic measurements collected during RL training (Figure 3). Our starting point is the SFT checkpoint, trained with GRPO [ 24 ] on outcome rewards. 

Tool use is under-attempted. A defining property of agentic reasoning, unlike conventional agentic RL [ 31 , 33 ], is that tool use is optional : the model can either produce a <tool_call> tag or skip straight to <answer> . This optionality makes action fragile under RL. We track the fraction of rollouts that contain at least one tool call over the course of RL. As shown in Figure 3a, which bins each question by its tool-using rollout count among 𝑁 = 8 , the corresponding rollout-level tool-use rate is roughly 27% at step 1 and stays in the 20–35% range over RL training, well below the thinking-only majority. GRPO exerts no pressure to grow the tool-using minority, and the behavior that distinguishes agentic from non-agentic reasoning remains under-represented in each group. 

When attempted, the tool-using subgroup is prone to being all-wrong. Within a GRPO group of 𝑁 rollouts for one question, we call the rollouts that emit a tool call the tool-using subgroup 

and the rest the no-tool subgroup , and the tool-using subgroup is all-wrong when none of its rollouts is correct, even if some no-tool rollouts in the same group succeed. Conditional on a question where tool use is attempted, how often does the tool-using subgroup fail together? Figure 3b shows this all-wrong rate is ∼40% for tool-using subgroups compared to ∼25% for no-tool subgroups. Under group-normalized advantage, this asymmetry yields non-positive advantage on tool-call tokens in both regimes: in mixed groups they trail the no-tool success signal, and in fully all-wrong groups the numerator 𝑟 𝑖 − mean ({𝑟 𝑗 }) collapses to zero. Either way, effective learning signal on tool calls is suppressed on the questions where tool use would matter most. 

Tool calls admit substantive resampling at a fixed thinking. Resampling at the tool call is only worthwhile if it is a real divergence point under the policy. If the thinking prefix already

• Resampling from a fixed prefix preserves substantive diversity in the tool call (2.9–3.4 semantic clusters per 16 samples in Figure 3c): the tool call is a real divergence point.

3.2. Resample candidate selection Trigger and budget allocation. AXPO resamples only on groups in which the tool-using subgroup is non-empty and entirely wrong (an all-wrong tool-using subgroup ; no-tool rollouts in the same group may succeed). These are exactly the groups where tool-call tokens receive non-positive advantage under GRPO (Section 2.2): zero when the entire group is all-wrong, negative when only no-tool rollouts succeed. A recovered continuation therefore delivers the largest gradient lift per resampled continuation. Proposition 1 would in principle admit groups with at least one correct tool-using rollout as well, but those already produce positive advantage on tool-call tokens and gain comparatively little from a marginal recovery. Resampling every triggered prefix would add up to 

𝐵𝑁 𝐾 continuations on top of 𝐵𝑁 standard rollouts (batch size 𝐵 ), so AXPO caps the extra cost up to 𝑟 · 𝐵𝑁 per step with 𝑟 < 1 and allocates breadth-first: every triggered question receives its top-ranked prefix before any receives a second. 

Uncertainty-based prefix ranking. Not every admitted prefix is worth resampling. Figure 3c shows ∼30% of prefixes collapse to a single cluster across 16 resamples. AXPO therefore ranks candidates by the mean policy probability over the tool-call tokens in 𝜏 src (a tractable uncertainty proxy [ 34 ]) and resamples the lowest-confidence prefixes first (see Appendix B.9 for the choice of confidence over predictive entropy).

## 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental setup Base models and tools. We use Qwen3-VL-Thinking [ 19 ] at three sizes (2B, 4B, 8B) as the base model across all methods. The tool set is the three families introduced in Section 2.1: a Python interpreter, a web search engine (Tavily search API 1), and an image zoom-in tool. All tools share a unified <tool_call> interface. Full prompt templates and tool schemas are in Appendix A.2. 

Training data & pipeline. SFT uses 64K correct trajectories drawn from ViRL [ 35 ], fvqa [ 36 ], and PyVision-RL [ 18 ], generated by Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking [ 19 ] where ∼25% contain a tool call. RL uses a 16K filtered subset plus 22K questions from MMFineReason-hard [ 37 ]. We follow the SFT then RL pipeline of Section 2.1. AXPO augments the rollout loop with tool-call resampling, holding all other hyperparameters constant against SFT + GRPO. Full details are in Appendix A.1. 

Benchmarks and metrics. We evaluate on nine multimodal benchmarks grouped into three task categories: Reasoning (MathVision [ 38 ], DynaMath [ 39 ], Math-VR [ 40 ]), Perception (V ⋆ [8], VisualProbe [ 7 ], HR-Bench-4K and HR-Bench-8K [ 41 ]), and Search (HR-MMSearch [ 42 ], MM-Search [ 43 ]); per-benchmark descriptions in Appendix A.3. All benchmarks are evaluated in the agentic setting with the same tool interface used in training. We report Pass@1 and Pass@4 (%), averaged over four rollouts per question, with answer matching logic tailored for each benchmark. 

Baselines. We compare AXPO against four baselines: (1) Base : the base model only with prompting without additional training (2) SFT : the SFT checkpoint without RL (3) GRPO : RL from the base model without an SFT stage and (4) SFT + GRPO : the standard SFT-then-RL recipe. Our method, SFT + AXPO : applies tool-call resampling and advantage calculation from Section 3 on top of the same SFT-then-RL pipeline.

4.2. Main Results: AXPO narrows the agentic gap to a 4 × larger baseline. 

Table 1 reports Pass@1 across nine benchmarks at four model sizes (per-benchmark Pass@4 results in Table 5, Appendix B.2). Decomposing the 8B Pass@1 column of Table 1, SFT + AXPO gains +0 .6 pp on Reasoning, +2 .9 pp on Perception, and +1 .3 pp on Search over the SFT + GRPO baseline. In relative terms, the gain concentrates in Perception ( +4% ) and is smallest on Reasoning ( +1% ). Perception is where image-tool use most directly determines correctness, while on Reasoning the bottleneck is often the thinking rather than the tool call. A detailed discussion of the results is provided in Appendix B.4.

## 5. Related Works 

Agentic reasoning for VLMs. Agentic reasoning extends the reasoning loop beyond the model’s token space, allowing it to emit a tool call, receive an external output, and condition subsequent reasoning on it [ 48 , 9]. Tools span knowledge search [ 5, 4 ], computation [ 10 , 11 , 49 –51 ], and visual perception [ 44 , 7, 12 , 45 , 52 , 53 ]. Recent VLM systems combine image manipulation with code and search [43, 23, 18, 14, 54], and follow a two-stage SFT-then-RL recipe. 

> 10 Agent Explorative Policy Optimization

RL for agentic reasoning. On thinking-only trajectories, group-relative methods like GRPO [ 24 –26 ] normalize rewards within 𝑁 rollouts and uniformly assign the resulting advantage; BroRL [ 30 ]analyzes how scaling 𝑁 closes coverage gaps. Three lines extend this family to tool-using rollouts: 

direct ports that reuse outcome-reward RL without changing the sampling structure [ 45 , 23 , 55 –58 , 22 , 42 ], mid-trajectory branching that resamples the continuation after a tool call to absorb tool-output uncertainty [ 31 , 59 ], and reward shaping that adds bonus or penalty terms on tool use [18, 29]. We include more detailed comparison with related works in Appendix C. 

Our contribution. AXPO contrasts with each line. Against direct use of GRPO, it restores learning signal on the all-wrong tool-using subgroups identified in Section 2.2, which uniform sampling cannot reach. Against mid-trajectory branching [ 31 , 59 ], it resamples at the tool-call boundary rather than after the tool observation, exploring the tool call itself rather than the continuation given a tool output. Against reward shaping, it acts on the rollout distribution while leaving the reward intact. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We identified a Thinking-Acting Gap in group-based agentic RL, an asymmetry between thinking (the self-contained default) and tool use (a high-variance auxiliary) that manifests during training as two symptoms: tool use is attempted on a minority of rollouts and tool-using subgroups (the tool-using rollouts within a group) are disproportionately all-wrong, so the advantage at tool-call tokens is non-positive on exactly the questions where tool use would help. AXPO narrows this gap by augmenting the rollout loop with tool-call resampling , which fixes the thinking prefix and concentrates exploration on the tool call itself, paired with uncertainty-based prefix selection. Across nine multimodal benchmarks and three model scales, AXPO consistently outperforms SFT + GRPO, and at 8B it surpasses the 32B Base baseline on Pass@4 with 4× fewer parameters. 

Limitations. Our study assumes verifiable outcome rewards for RL and trains models up to 8B parameters. We discuss limitations and the future directions in details in Appendix E.

## A. Experimental Setup 

This section expands on Section 4.1: training data and hyperparameters (Appendix A.1), the agentic system prompt and tool schemas (Appendix A.2), and per-benchmark descriptions (Appendix A.3). 

A.1. Training 

We expand on Section 4.1 with the full training data composition and hyperparameter list. 

SFT data composition. SFT uses 64,274 trajectories drawn from three sources: ViRL [ 35 ], fvqa [ 36 ], and PyVision-RL [ 18 ]. All trajectories are generated by Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking [ 19 ]acting as the teacher model. ∼25% of the trajectories contain at least one tool call; the remainder solve the problem with internal reasoning alone. We retain only trajectories whose final answer is correct against the ground-truth label. 

RL data composition. RL uses 15,591 problems obtained by filtering the SFT problem set: we remove problems that the SFT checkpoint solves on all 4 rollouts, and problems that the 32B teacher fails on all 4 rollouts. This filtering convention follows prior agentic RL work [ 23 , 26 ] and removes both trivial problems (zero learning signal) and unreachable problems (no positive signal achievable at this scale). We additionally include 22,000 hard questions from MMFineReason-hard 2 to expose the policy to problems unseen during SFT, bringing the RL training set to ∼37K problems. 

Policy gradient objective. A.2. System Prompt and Tool Interface 

We use the same agentic system prompt for SFT, RL, and evaluation across all model sizes where the prompt is modified version of the system prompt of deepeyes v2 [ 23 ]. Tools are exposed via a unified <tool_call> interface used in qwen-agent [ 19 ] and the model is free to invoke any subset of the three tools ( python , tavily-search , image_zoom_in ) on every question. The full text of the prompt is reproduced below. 

> System Prompt

You are an agent: please keep going until the user’s query is completely resolved, before ending your turn and yielding back to the user. Only terminate your turn when you are sure that the problem is solved. Solve the following problem step by step. In your reasoning process, if the answer cannot be determined, you can write Python code in a Jupyter Notebook to process the image and extract more information from it. The stdout and stderr content, along with the images generated by “plt.show() ” will be returned to better assist with the user query. You MUST use the python tool to analyze or transform images whenever it could improve your understanding. This includes but is not limited to zooming in, rotating, adjusting contrast, computing statistics, or isolating features. If you find you lack sufficient knowledge to confidently answer the question, you MUST conduct search to thoroughly seek the internet for information. No matter how complex the query, you will not give up until you find the corresponding information. You can use the tavily-search tool, which will search the web and return relevant information based on your query.

A.3. Benchmark Details 

We evaluate on nine multimodal benchmarks grouped by which tool family is most expected to help: Reasoning (Python interpreter), Perception (image zoom-in), and Search (web search). All benchmarks are evaluated in the agentic setting with the same tool interface used in training; the model is free to use any subset of the three tools or none at all on every question. 

Reasoning. These benchmarks evaluate multi-step reasoning over text, math, and figures, where a Python interpreter is the natural tool for symbolic and numerical computation. 

• MathVision [ 38 ]: competition-style math problems paired with diagrams (geometry, algebra, combinatorics). Answers are short numerical or symbolic expressions; we use the public mini test set containing 304 questions. 

• DynaMath [ 39 ]: a benchmark of programmatically generated math questions with multiple parametric variants per seed problem. We evaluate on a single-variant slice (501 seed problems). The benchmark complements MathVision as it provides additional algorithmically generated problems spanning plane geometry, statistics, and other domains with diverse difficulty. 

• Math-VR [ 40 ]: a mathematical visual reasoning benchmark introduced with CodePlot-CoT, in which problems are paired with diagrams (figures, plots, geometric constructions) that the model must inspect to compute the answer. The benchmark targets the regime where the visual content is load-bearing for the math derivation rather than decorative. 

Perception. These benchmarks contain high-resolution images with information that is hard to read at the default vision encoder resolution. The image zoom-in tool is the natural recovery mechanism. 

• V⋆ [8]: a fine-grained visual-search benchmark in which the answer depends on a small object embedded in a complex scene. Designed to expose the failure of monolithic image encoders on high-resolution detail. 

> 25 Agent Explorative Policy Optimization

• VisualProbe [7]: a multimodal probing benchmark introduced with mini-o3, where each question targets a specific small region (text, sign, label) within a high-resolution scene. We use the hard 

subset, which selects questions Qwen3-VL-Thinking-8B fails without tools. 

• HR-Bench-4K and HR-Bench-8K [ 41 ]: high-resolution single-image perception at 4096 2 and 

8192 2 resolutions, respectively. Questions probe small text, distant objects, and fine textures; the two splits share a common question pool with images rescaled to the target resolution. 

Search. These benchmarks require external knowledge that is unlikely to be in the model’s parametric memory or that requires multi-hop lookup. The Tavily web search API is the natural tool. 

• HR-MMSearch [ 42 ]: high-resolution multimodal search questions whose answers are composed by chaining two or more web lookups (e.g., “in which year was the mayor of the city where this fashion designer was born”). The benchmark stresses both perception (identifying entities from the image) and multi-hop search composition. 

• MMSearch [43 ]: an earlier multimodal search benchmark covering current-events questions whose answers depend on web content released after the model’s training cutoff. Predominantly single-hop, but answers are often time-sensitive. 

Evaluation protocol. For all benchmarks, we draw four rollouts per question and report Pass@1 (per-question accuracy averaged over four rollouts) and Pass@4 (a question is correct if any of the four rollouts is correct). Final-answer matching against the gold label is performed by an exact match for math and multiple-choice question and F1 score for search questions. For benchmarks that are hard to evaluate with exact match (e.g., MMSearch), we use a llm-as-a-judge using gpt-5-mini model.

RL from the base policy can find a single high-reward mode (Pass@1 partially recovered at 8B), but it consistently sacrifices diversity, capping the Pass@4 ceiling [ 62 ]. SFT broadens the cold-start distribution over tool-using trajectories before group-based exploration begins. Without it, all-wrong tool-using subgroups dominate from step 1 and the policy collapses onto a narrow single-mode solution [23]. 

Why RL after SFT? SFT minimizes log-likelihood on a fixed set of teacher trajectories: it can make the student imitate the teacher’s tool-use patterns, but the optimization signal lives entirely on the teacher’s distribution. At test time the student samples from its own distribution and small token-level deviations from the teacher accumulate into prefixes the SFT objective never scored, so imitation alone does not guarantee that on-policy rollouts inherit the teacher’s correctness (exposure bias ). RL after SFT narrows this gap by training on the policy’s own rollouts under an outcome reward, adapting the policy to its own induced trajectory distribution rather than to the teacher’s [ 63 ]. Empirically, comparing SFT against SFT + GRPO isolates this on-policy adaptation effect given the SFT initialization: Pass@1 improves at every scale ( +4 .5 at 2B, +1 .2 at 4B, +2 .0

at 8B), the regime where on-policy reliability matters most, while Pass@4 gains are smaller and uneven ( +2 .3, +0 .0, +1 .8), consistent with GRPO consolidating mass on trajectories the SFT policy can already reach rather than discovering new ones. RL is therefore necessary on top of SFT, but standard GRPO’s adaptation is biased toward the SFT manifold, motivating AXPO’s tool-call resampling.

Table 8 shows the result. Without image search at inference, SFT + GRPO and SFT + AXPO are essentially tied (Pass@1: 44 .0 vs. 45 .0; Pass@4: 59 .3 vs. 61 .0). With image search at inference, the gap opens on Pass@1: AXPO gains +4 .6 pp ( 45 .0 → 49 .6) while GRPO gains only +2 .8 pp (44 .0 → 46 .8); on Pass@4 both methods gain similarly ( +6 .0 and +5 .7). The Pass@1 asymmetry suggests AXPO not only can use the unseen tool when given enough samples (Pass@4 captures this) but converts that capability into reliable first-shot answers, whereas GRPO’s tool-using policy is more narrowly fitted to the training tool inventory. We read this as evidence that resampling-induced exposure to diverse tool-call resolutions during training transfers to tool families the model has never been trained on, although the claim is bounded to one benchmark and one new tool type.

B.9. Confidence as a Tractable Proxy for Tool-Call Entropy 

Section 3 ranks candidate prefixes by the mean probability the source policy assigned to the tool-call tokens of 𝜏 src (henceforth confidence ). The natural alternative is the predictive entropy 

𝐻 (𝜋 𝜃 (· | 𝑥 , 𝑦 <𝑡 )) of the next-token distribution at each tool-call position, but this choice is precluded by training-loop structure. Per-token sampling probabilities are emitted essentially for free by the rollout engine (vLLM [ 65 ]), whereas predictive entropy requires either an extra forward pass over the full vocabulary under the trained policy (FSDP) or vLLM-side full-vocabulary logprob expansion at decode time. Our RL pipeline under verl [ 60 ] alternates rollout (vLLM) and gradient updates (FSDP), so inserting a per-prefix entropy computation would force an additional vLLM → FSDP → vLLM round-trip per training step (or equivalently expensive vLLM-side full-vocabulary logprob expansion), a cost we wished to avoid. To verify that confidence preserves the ranking an entropy-based scheme would induce, we measured the per-step correlation between mean-token-confidence and exact policy entropy on tool-call positions of failed tool-using rollouts collected during training (Figure 6). Across training steps, the two signals are strongly correlated (Pearson 𝜌 = 0 .843 , Spearman 𝜌 = 0 .835 , OLS fit 𝑦 = 0 .41 𝑥 + 0 .07 on entropy vs negated confidence). Confidence therefore preserves the relative ordering an entropy-based scheme would produce, and we adopt it as the ranking signal in AXPO.

Group-relative RL and BroRL. RL with verifiable outcome rewards is the standard post-SFT stage for reasoning models [ 1, 2 ]. ## E. Limitations and Future Work 

We expand on the limitations sketched in the main-text conclusion and discuss future directions. 

Verifiable outcome rewards. AXPO’s subgroup-level trigger (“all-wrong tool-using subgroup”) and per-prefix advantage in Eq. 2 both rely on a binary, automatically verifiable outcome signal 

𝑟 ∈ { 0, 1}. Tasks where verifiability is partial (open-ended generation [ 67 , 68 ], multi-criterion subjective evaluation) require a different definition of “failed subgroup” before tool-call resampling applies. The resampling mechanism itself is reward-agnostic, but the trigger and the all-wrong learning signal argument in Section 2.2 are not. 

Compute scope of training. Trained models are at most 8B parameters; 32B is reported inference-only as the SFT teacher. This boundary reflects available compute rather than a methodological choice [ 69 , 68 , 70 –72 ]. Whether the Thinking-Acting Gap and AXPO’s recovery generalize to 32B+ trainable scale, where the base policy is closer to the tool-use ceiling and the all-wrong-subgroup fraction may be smaller, is open. Furthermore, although the teacher model can theoretically scale to 100B+ parameters, analyzing the effects of teacher scaling on downstream reinforcement learning (RL) remains an area for future work. 

Future work: richer tool environments. Our tool inventory covers three families that span numerical, retrieval, and visual modalities (Python interpreter, web search, image zoom-in), each shown to be load-bearing in prior agentic VLM work [ 23 , 18 , 7]; this multi-tool setup is already broader than most prior agentic-VLM RL studies, which typically focus on a single tool family. One notable absence is an image-search tool, which prior works [ 23 , 42 ] report results with but which we could not include during training and full evaluation due to per-call API costs at our scale (we instead approximate it via a GPT-5.4 prompt for the unseen-tool study in Appendix B.5). Tools with longer execution latency (browser agents, GUI control, terminal sessions) and tools with 

> 36 Agent Explorative Policy Optimization

high per-call cost (LLM-as-tool) [ 13 ] introduce additional considerations that we do not address. Extending AXPO to such richer [73–75], longer-horizon environments is a natural next step. 

Future work: generalization to other base models. All experiments use Qwen3-VL-Thinking, which is currently the strongest open thinking-capable VLM with reliable tool-using ability and the model best supported by our RL framework. The Thinking-Acting Gap is a property of RL on an agentic reasoning model rather than of any specific architecture [ 76 – 79 ], and we expect the diagnostics and AXPO’s coverage gain to transfer; replicating on the most recent Qwen3.5 models [ 80 ], once RL framework support matures, would help establish the gap as a general phenomenon and rule out base-specific confounds. 

## F. Broader Impacts 

AXPO is a methodological improvement to the RL training of agentic VLMs and is additive on top of a publicly released base model (Qwen3-VL-Thinking [ 19 ]), so it does not introduce a new capability class. Among the three tool families used in this work, the Python interpreter raises standard security concerns for code-executing agents. We can run all tool calls in a sandboxed execution environment during both training and evaluation, consistent with prior agentic-VLM RL work [ 12 ]. Beyond this sandboxing, broader safety considerations [ 81 –87 ] (e.g., misuse of tool-grounded outputs, causality, robustness, deployment-time guardrails, downstream effects of cheaper agentic deployments) are out of scope for this work and were not addressed.
