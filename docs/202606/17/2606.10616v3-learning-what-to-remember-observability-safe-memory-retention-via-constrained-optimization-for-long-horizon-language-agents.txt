## ABSTRACT 

Long-horizon language agents accumulate observations and facts that exceed their context windows, making memory retention—deciding what to keep, discard, or later recover under a fixed budget—fundamental to sustained performance. Existing systems score memories with local heuristics such as recency or rele-vance, ignoring delayed consequences: future retrieval failures, recomputation, and stale-information use. We formulate memory retention as a constrained, par-tially observable stochastic optimization problem with explicit state transitions, budget constraints, and delayed costs. Because exact optimization of this multi-step problem is NP-hard, and future demands are unknown at decision time, we develop OSL-MR (Observability-Safe Learning for Memory Retention), a learning-augmented approximation framework. Its central principle is observabil-ity separation: deployment decisions use only online-observable signals, while supervision derived from realized evidence is used exclusively for offline pol-icy learning. OSL-MR pairs a budget-aware Mixed-Score heuristic, which en-ables cold-start deployment, with an evidence learner that predicts which mem-ories will later serve as evidence. Across LoCoMo and LongMemEval, OSL-MR consistently outperforms strong heuristic and imitation-learning baselines, particularly under tight budgets, and remains robust across cost settings. The Mixed-Score prior improves precision and modestly improves recall. On exactly-solvable instances, we show that single-step optimization is insufficient to antic-ipate future demand shifts: even a perfect single-step optimizer leaves a substan-tial optimality gap, whereas OSL-MR stays significantly closer to the dynamic-programming optimum. These results establish constrained stochastic optimiza-tion and optimization-guided learning as a principled foundation for memory man-agement in long-horizon agents. 

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Large language model (LLM) agents increasingly operate over long horizons, requiring them to decide which memories to retain, evict, or reacquire under limited context and cost budgets (Hu et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2026). These decisions are not isolated: each one shapes future informa-tion availability, retrieval quality, and downstream performance. Memory management is therefore a sequential decision problem under uncertainty, in which actions have delayed, state-dependent consequences. Most existing memory systems rely on heuristic scoring, retrieval optimization, or learned compres-sion (Park et al., 2023; Packer et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023), but treat retention

They lack a principled for-mulation of the long-horizon consequences—missing future evidence, reacquisition cost, and stale information under partial observability—and do not specify what an optimal retention policy should achieve under limited capacity and delayed feedback. 

We fill this gap by formulating memory retention as a constrained stochastic optimization problem that explicitly accounts for budget feasibility, evidence utility, and delayed costs including miss penalty, reacquisition delay, and stale-information risk. Rooted in classical operations research (OR), this formulation views retention as sequential resource allocation under uncertainty, with a hard budget and outcome-dependent costs that materialize only in the future. To our knowledge, prior work—whether based on heuristic scoring, retrieval optimization, or reinforcement learning (RL)—proposes solutions without first defining what optimal retention means under budget con-straints, delayed consequences, and partial observability; we provide such a formulation for the first time, and a learning objective follows naturally from it. The closest optimization-based approach, Fofadiya & Tiwari (Fofadiya & Tiwari, 2026), treats retention as a single-step problem that opti-mizes immediate relevance, ignoring how current decisions affect future evidence availability and incur delayed penalties, and thus misses the essential long-horizon nature of the problem. A central challenge in operationalizing this formulation is observability: many signals needed to judge a retention decision—gold evidence, answer correctness, semantic freshness—are available only after the decision is made. Using them at deployment would create an unrealistic information advantage. We therefore enforce a strict separation between online-observable features (query con-text, memory metadata, interaction history) and offline-available supervision (OAS) (gold evidence, answer text, ground-truth freshness). OAS is used only for training and evaluation, while deployable policies rely solely on online inputs. This discipline guarantees that any policy learned under it can be deployed without oracle access to future information, making it suitable for online interactive agents. Building on this foundation, we propose OSL-MR (Observability-Safe Learning for Memory 

Retention). Because the retention problem is computationally intractable—its single-step version already generalizes budgeted maximum coverage (Proposition 1)—and future information demands are unknown at decision time, exact optimization is infeasible. OSL-MR therefore provides a 

learning-augmented approximation framework with two complementary components: (i) an evi-dence learner trained offline from interaction logs using supervision derived from realized evidence, and (ii) a Mixed-Score heuristic that serves as both a cold-start deployable baseline and an online-safe feasibility prior. Deployment is staged: Mixed-Score runs alone from the first user query while all interactions are logged; once sufficient data accumulate, the evidence learner is trained offline and deployed as a frozen policy, replacing the heuristic without violating observability. By learning query-conditioned evidence scores directly from gold labels, OSL-MR bypasses general-purpose importance oracles; feeding the Mixed-Score value to the learner as an additional feature acts as an inductive prior that, as our ablations confirm, improves precision without sacrificing recall. A crucial design choice is to learn from evidence-membership signals rather than from direct reward. The reward is inherently non-decomposable and combinatorial—retention quality depends on sets of memories, not individual items—so assigning reward to individual decisions is ill-posed and computationally intractable. Evidence membership instead provides a tractable per-memory signal that aligns with the structure of the optimization objective while remaining fully compatible with online deployment. Our contributions are fourfold. First , we formalize memory retention as a constrained sequen-tial decision problem under a hard budget—modeling evidence utility, storage cost, miss penalty, reacquisition delay, and stale risk—and prove that the full multi-step problem is NP-hard (Proposi-tion 1), establishing the need for approximation. Second , we introduce OSL-MR, an observability-safe learning framework that enforces a strict online/OAS separation and integrates the Mixed-Score prior, the optimization formulation, and an evidence learner trained from direct evidence supervi-sion. Third , on two public long-horizon benchmarks, LoCoMo (Maharana et al., 2024) and Long-MemEval (Wu et al., 2025), OSL-MR consistently outperforms recency-based methods, Generative Agents-style scoring, and other heuristic baselines, especially under tight budgets; the Mixed-Score prior improves precision and modestly improves recall, and sensitivity analysis confirms robust-ness across cost configurations. Fourth , on small, exactly-solvable instances of the same problem, we quantify optimality gaps against the dynamic-programming optimum. These experiments show 2Preprint that single-step optimization is insufficient to anticipate future demand shifts: a perfect single-step optimizer is far from optimal under shifting demand, while OSL-MR stays close to the optimum, providing direct evidence for the value of the sequential formulation and the near-optimality of our approximation. 

## 2 RELATED WORK 

2.1 MEMORY SYSTEMS AND LONG -H ORIZON LANGUAGE AGENTS 

Long-horizon language agents rely on external memory to extend their effective context. Early sys-tems store episodic experiences or tool traces in vector databases; MemGPT (Packer et al., 2023) introduces operating-system-inspired hierarchical paging, while MemoryBank (Zhong et al., 2024) adds Ebbinghaus-style forgetting to retrieval. Generative Agents (Park et al., 2023) rank memories by recency, relevance, and importance, but their static importance scores capture general salience rather than query-specific evidence value, limiting effectiveness under capacity constraints. Other systems extend different stages of the memory lifecycle: Mem0 (Chhikara et al., 2025) structures memory writing, MEM1 (Zhou et al., 2025) learns compact latent representations via RL, and prompt compression (Jiang et al., 2023) reduces context cost. Across these, retention is handled implicitly within retrieval or compression pipelines; to our knowledge no prior work formulates it as an explicit long-horizon optimization under budget constraints and partial observability—the gap we address. 2.2 MEMORY RETENTION AS RESOURCE ALLOCATION 

Memory retention is a resource-allocation problem under uncertainty, classically modeled in oper-ations research as constrained optimization that trades off immediate gains against future penalties; surveys (Hu et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2026) note the absence of such unified formulations for re-tention. Several works add optimization for cost-constrained retrieval (e.g., AdaGReS (Peng et al., 2025), CORAG (Wang et al., 2024)), but target one-turn context selection rather than long-horizon retention. For retention itself, Fofadiya and Tiwari (Fofadiya & Tiwari, 2026) formulate a single-step budgeted optimization that maximizes immediate relevance independently at each step, ignoring how current decisions affect future evidence availability or incur delayed penalties. At its combinatorial core, the per-step retention subproblem generalizes budgeted maximum cov-erage, a classical NP-hard problem (Khuller et al., 1999; Feige, 1998); we make this precise in Proposition 1. Because the coverage objective is monotone and submodular, a cost-effective greedy attains the well-known (1 − 1/e ) guarantee when the coverage sets are known (Nemhauser et al., 1978; Khuller et al., 1999; Feige, 1998). This places retention within the well-studied tradition of submodular maximization under knapsack constraints and clarifies our stance: at deployment the coverage sets are unobservable, so the guarantee is not directly attainable, which is exactly why we pursue a greedy, learning-augmented approximation that estimates evidence value from logged supervision rather than solving the problem exactly. Classical OR methods such as integer or dynamic programming do not apply directly here: the problem is partially observable—the distribution of future evidence demands depends on unknown user queries—and the state space is combinatorial. On deliberately reduced instances, however, it becomes exactly solvable by dynamic programming, which we exploit in Section 4.3 to obtain ground-truth optima and measure how near-optimal a deployable policy can be. At benchmark scale exact optimization is out of reach, so we adopt a learning-based approximation: OSL-MR mod-els retention as a multi-step sequential decision problem and optimizes cumulative reward over the full horizon. By contrast, BudgetMem approaches (Alla et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2026a), Mem-T (Yue et al., 2026), and MemAct (Zhang et al., 2025) operate as one-step decisions. 2.3 LEARNING -B ASED MEMORY POLICIES AND OBSERVABILITY SEPARATION 

Recent learning-based systems optimize memory policies using downstream signals: Mem-α (Wang et al., 2025) applies RL to learn memory construction, Acon (Kang et al., 2025) optimizes context compression for long-horizon agents, and MemRL (Zhang et al., 2026b) frames retrieval as a value-based decision updated from environmental feedback. These improve retrieval or compression rather than retention under hard budgets. The STALE benchmark (Chao et al., 2026) shows that LLM agents struggle to detect when a stored memory has become outdated, motivating a separation be-tween observable temporal signals and latent semantic validity—precisely our online/OAS principle. Whereas many learning methods rely on supervision unavailable at deployment, OSL-MR uses gold evidence only during offline training and restricts deployed policies to online-observable features, preserving validity under partial observability. Our framework is complementary to retrieval-side optimization (e.g., MemRL); unifying retention and retrieval under a single constrained optimiza-tion formulation is a promising direction for future work. 

## 3 METHODOLOGY 

We propose OSL-MR, a memory retention framework for long-horizon language agents under strict budget constraints and partial observability. Memory decisions are made sequentially with limited capacity, yet their consequences—information loss, recomputation cost, and stale usage—surface only in the future, which makes naive heuristic rules or local scoring strategies insufficient for this delayed, partially observable problem. OSL-MR addresses this by formulating memory retention as a constrained partially observable stochastic optimization problem under an explicit observability separation (Section 3.1). The frame-work integrates three tightly coupled components, elaborated in the following subsections: (i) a constrained optimization formulation that defines long-horizon retention objectives under budget limitations (Section 3.2), (ii) a Mixed-Score heuristic that provides a fully deployable cold-start so-lution and a strong inductive baseline (Section 3.3), and (iii) an evidence learner trained offline from interaction logs that refines the heuristic policy using supervision derived from realized evidence (Section 3.4). Section 3.5 discusses a behavior-cloning variant for comparison. Figure 1 illustrates the overall framework and its data flow. 3.1 OBSERVABILITY SEPARATION 

We first formalize the information asymmetry in memory retention, distinguishing three types of signals. Online-observable inputs include the current query, memory metadata, and interaction context, available at decision time. Offline-available supervision (OAS) includes gold evidence sets, answer content, coverage signals, and downstream outcome statistics, accessible only after the interaction completes. Evaluation signals are derived from realized system outcomes and used strictly for analysis and benchmarking. A key constraint in OSL-MR is that deployed policies are strictly restricted to online-observable inputs. This ensures that inference-time decisions are realistic and do not rely on oracle informa-tion, so any improvement must arise from better generalization over observable features rather than leakage of supervision. 4Preprint

Multi-step structure and trade-offs. Because the retained store carries over through Ct = At and shapes the future pool Mt+1 = Ct ∪ Nt+1 , the formulation optimizes cumulative reward over the whole horizon and is a genuine multi-step problem: a hard budget forces selective retention, while delayed penalties (miss, reacquisition, staleness) materialize only in future interactions, so the policy must anticipate future evidence needs and weigh short-term gains against long-term costs. A single-step optimizer, by contrast, maximizes only the immediate reward rt and may discard a memory that looks irrelevant now but becomes critical later, incurring avoidable miss or reacquisition costs. The deployed OSL-MR policy itself makes a state-dependent decision at each step—its candidate pool and history features depend on past actions—and is trained from sequential logs and evaluated on cumulative reward; it is thus an online approximation to the sequential optimum rather than an exact planner, whose closeness to the true optimum we quantify on small, exactly-solvable instances in Section 4.3. 

From intractability to learning-based approximation. This constrained partially observable stochastic optimization admits no closed-form or standard-solver solution: future evidence demands depend on unknown user queries, rewards involve delayed outcomes not observable at decision time, and the state space is combinatorial in the number of memories. We prove that even the single-step version is NP-hard via reduction from budgeted maximum coverage (Proposition 1). Because the multi-step problem contains the single-step case as a special instance (T=1), the full multi-step problem is NP-hard as well. Hardness persists under partial observability, which only restricts the policy’s information without simplifying the underlying combinatorial selection. We therefore learn a retention policy from interaction logs. Since no logs exist initially, we first deploy a lightweight, budget-aware heuristic (Mixed-Score), intentionally stronger than naive baselines such as recency 6Preprint or Generative Agents, that keeps the system operational from the first query while logging all user-agent interactions. Once enough logs accumulate, we train an evidence learner offline under the same observability constraints to approximate the optimal policy. We additionally feed the Mixed-Score value to the learner as an extra input feature, which acts as an inductive prior that improves precision and modestly improves recall (ablation in Section 4). Mixed-Score thus plays two roles— cold-start deployment and a feature prior—while the learner remains the core performance driver, circumventing unobservability by exploiting offline evidence labels. 

Challenge of reward supervision. One might instead train a model directly on the per-step or cumulative reward. However, reward is a sequence-level scalar that cannot be decomposed into per-memory credit: a miss penalty occurs when a future query’s evidence is missing, but which spe-cific discarded memory caused it is ambiguous when several memories support the same evidence. Obtaining reward labels would moreover require evaluating every retained subset At ⊆ Mt—a combinatorial space of size 2|Mt|. For comparison, we construct a behavior-cloning baseline that learns from a non-deployable teacher policy built using offline-available supervision (OAS). For each training instance, starting from the Mixed-Score selection, the teacher performs a local greedy search: it considers swapping one re-tained memory with a currently omitted candidate, evaluates the resulting retained set using the reward of Section 3.2 (which depends on gold evidence), and accepts the swap if it improves the reward, continuing until no improvement is found. The teacher thus produces a higher-quality re-tained set Ateacher t than the original Mixed-Score, but relies on OAS and is therefore unsuitable for deployment. The pseudo-labels for behavior cloning are defined as yBC t,i = 1[mt,i ∈ Ateacher t ]. The BC-learner is trained using the same architecture and loss as the evidence learner, but with these pseudo-labels instead of gold evidence labels, and uses the same online-observable features ψon t,i (including the stale-risk signal stale risk t,i ) as input. This variant evaluates whether imitating an improved heuris-tic policy can substitute for direct evidence supervision. 

## 4 NUMERICAL EXPERIMENTS 

4.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 

Online/OAS discipline. All experiments strictly follow the observability separation defined in Sec-tion 3. Deployable policies are restricted to online-observable inputs, including query context, mem-ory metadata, recency, question overlap, entity overlap, session/speaker information, and cost fea-tures. Offline-available supervision (OAS) includes gold evidence, answer text, realized coverage, miss events, reacquisition cost, and ground-truth freshness. OAS is used only for training or evalua-tion of oracle/teacher variants and is never accessible to deployable policies. Any method that relies on OAS at inference time is treated as an oracle baseline, not a deployable policy.

Compared methods. We compare against several existing approaches and variants: • Recency : A practical and widely used heuristic that retains the top-K most recent mem-ories by timestamp, where K is the maximum number of items that fit under the budget. This approach is simple, computationally efficient, and commonly employed as a baseline despite ignoring query relevance or evidence utility. • Generative Agents (GA) : A widely adopted retrieval paradigm that scores each memory as a weighted sum of recency, relevance, and importance (Park et al., 2023). We evaluate two importance variants: GA-heuristic (hand-crafted rules based on memory content, e.g., higher weight for emotional events) and GA-LLM (importance scores queried from an LLM offline). Both variants share the same recency and relevance computation. • Mixed-Score : Our proposed online-safe heuristic prior. It computes a utility score for each memory by combining multiple observable feature groups (temporal, relevance, context, risk) with a size penalty term. The score is used to select the top-scoring memories under the budget. Mixed-Score is fully deployable and serves as a strong baseline as well as an inductive prior for learning. • BC-learner : A behavior-cloning baseline trained to imitate the retention decisions of a non-deployable teacher policy. The teacher performs local greedy search around the 10 Preprint Mixed-Score selection using oracle gold evidence (OAS) to produce higher-quality re-tained sets. BC-learner uses the same architecture as OSL-MR but is supervised by teacher pseudo-labels instead of gold evidence. • OSL-MR (full) : Our complete method, which trains an evidence learner from logged in-teraction data using gold evidence labels. The learner takes online-observable features concatenated with the Mixed-Score prior as input, and outputs a utility logit per memory. At inference, a budget-constrained decoder selects the retained subset. The learned policy is frozen after training and uses no OAS at test time. • OSL-MR (w/o prior) : An ablation variant of OSL-MR that removes the Mixed-Score prior from the feature vector, using only online-observable features ψon . All other settings (training data, architecture, loss) remain identical, isolating the contribution of the prior. 

Note on single-step methods. Fofadiya and Tiwari (Fofadiya & Tiwari, 2026) formulate single-step budgeted optimization that maximizes immediate relevance per step, but they ignore delayed consequences (miss, reacquisition, staleness) and partial observability, and their code is not pub-licly available. Hence we do not include them as a quantitative baseline. Instead, our experiments include strong local heuristics (Recency, GA, Mixed-Score) that act as myopic, per-step decision policies; crucially, they are evaluated under the same sequential protocol described above, so that the comparison isolates the value of horizon-aware retention while keeping the evaluation dynamics identical across methods. In addition, we do not rely on these heuristics alone to characterize single-step optimization: in the controlled study of Section 4.3 we evaluate the exact single-step optimizer (Myopic)—the strongest policy any single-step method can attain—and find that it leaves a large optimality gap ( 19% –32% ) once demand is temporally coupled. This indicates that the limitation of single-step retention is intrinsic to the objective itself rather than specific to any one heuristic or implementation.

The proposed method OSL-MR substantially outperforms heuristics. Our primary evaluation is on the LoCoMo and LongMemEval benchmarks (Section 4.2), where OSL-MR is the best-performing deployable method. Building on those results, we now ask a com-plementary and more refined question: in absolute terms, how close to optimal is the learned policy, and how much does the multi-step structure of the problem contribute to its advantage? These questions cannot be answered on the benchmarks themselves. Because retention is a genuinely multi-step problem (Section 3), the benchmark instances are long-horizon and the optimal policy is computationally intractable to obtain—the problem is NP-hard (Proposition 1)—so there is no exact optimum on LoCoMo or LongMemEval to serve as a reference point. We therefore complement the main results with a controlled study on smaller, exactly-solvable instances, where the true optimum is available and the absolute quality of the learned policy can be measured directly. Concretely, we construct small instances of the same sequential decision process of Section 3—a per-step storage budget, evidence hits, misses served by a delayed and costly reacquisition channel, and a retained store carried over between steps ( Ct = At)—cast as a finite Markov decision process (MDP) that is small enough to solve exactly by dynamic programming (DP). On these instances we can compute both the true optimum V ⋆ and the exact single-step optimum, which lets us analyze two things directly: (i) how much the multi-step structure matters , by measuring how far even a perfect 

single-step optimizer falls short of the optimum; and (ii) how near-optimal , in absolute terms, our deployable learning-based policy is. Together these analyses provide direct, quantitative support for casting retention as a sequential problem and for approximating it by learning. 

Setup. Each instance has n = 6 candidate memories, horizon T = 9 , and budget B = 3 ; memory values and sizes are drawn from the same generator as our synthetic simulator, and per-step demand follows a categorical distribution that is either stationary or shifts between phases. The candidate pool is sticky: a memory dropped from the cache can re-enter only through the reacquisition channel (modeled with a delay and a per-item cost, as in Section 3), so a memory discarded before its demand rises must be costly reacquired later. All compared policies observe only the current-step demand at inference, and they fall into two classes that differ in how (if at all) they account for future demand. The first class are model-based planners : they have access to the known MDP dynamics and solve it by dynamic programming (DP); they are not deployable and serve only as reference points. The second class are deployable policies : they do no planning and use only online-observable features. The methods are: • Optimal (DP) : the exact optimum of the MDP, computed by full-horizon dynamic programming—backward induction over the reachable states, at each state enumerating the budget-feasible actions and taking expectations over the demand distribution. • Lookahead-d: a receding-horizon (model-predictive) planner that runs the same DP recur-sion but truncated to a d-step window. At each step it solves a d-step DP over the known dynamics (with zero terminal value beyond the window), commits only the first action, ad-vances one step, and re-plans. The lookahead depth d is the only thing that changes across these planners: d=1 is Myopic (1-step) , which optimizes the immediate reward alone (the exact single-step optimizer); larger d (2, 4) folds more of the future into the DP and shrinks the gap; and once d reaches the remaining horizon the planner coincides with Optimal (DP) . The Myopic → Lookahead-2 → Lookahead-4 → Optimal sequence is thus a single DP algorithm run at increasing foresight, which isolates exactly how much pure planning depth is worth. • Mixed-Score : the deployable greedy heuristic of Section 3.3, which ranks memories by their online cost-penalized utility score and greedily fills the budget. It performs no plan-ning and never expands the transition model, scoring memories from the current step only. • OSL-MR (learned) : a lightweight counterpart of our evidence learner (Section 3). It scores each memory from online-observable features together with the Mixed-Score prior, is trained offline on evidence-derived supervision (realized only after the interaction), and is then frozen and deployed with the same budget-aware greedy selection—using only on-line features and, like Mixed-Score, no planning. To respect the online/OAS separation, it is trained on a disjoint set of instances and evaluated frozen on the held-out test seeds. 15 Preprint Importantly, the lookahead depth d governs only how each planner decides its action; the expected return reported for every policy—planners and deployable heuristics alike—is then computed ex-actly by full-horizon dynamic-programming policy evaluation (no Monte-Carlo sampling), so all numbers are directly comparable to V ⋆. The comparison set here is deliberately different from the benchmark study (Section 4.2), because the two experiments answer different questions. The benchmark study compares many deployable methods—Recency, Generative Agents, and the BC-learner—on real data, but none of them belongs in this controlled study, for two related reasons. First, they are all defined through textual content and learned semantics (timestamps, LLM-assigned importance, entity overlap, or imitation of a teacher built from gold evidence), which have no coun-terpart in the abstract numerical MDP solved here and therefore cannot be ported to it. Second, the role each of them plays is already accounted for: the single-step heuristics (Recency, Generative Agents) are all dominated by Myopic, which is the strongest possible single-step policy and hence subsumes their best case, while the BC-learner exists only to test whether imitating a teacher can replace direct evidence supervision—a question already settled on the real benchmarks. What this study needs instead are the reference points that become available only at small scale: the exact op-timum V ⋆ and the lookahead spectrum running from the exact single-step optimum (Myopic) up to deeper planners. We keep Mixed-Score alongside them because it is the deployable cost-penalized greedy that OSL-MR generalizes and the one heuristic that transfers naturally to the abstract in-stances, which makes it the right reference for isolating the learner’s added value. retention quality and not merely for a favorable cost trade-off: on the benchmarks the reward ranking aligns with the evidence-quality metrics (precision, recall, and F1; Section 4.2), so a small reward gap also corresponds to near-best evidence retention. The two studies are thus complementary—the benchmarks establish that the reward objective tracks real retention quality, and this controlled study measures how close to that objective’s optimum each policy comes. 

Scope and representativeness of the instances. These instances are not an arbitrary toy. They instantiate the same reward components, per-step budget constraint, and state carry-over ( Ct = At)as the full model of Section 3, scaled down only enough that the optimum becomes computable. The two demand regimes capture precisely the structural feature that makes retention multi-step: in long conversations the relevant topics drift over time (shifting demand), and a memory discarded too early can be recovered only at a delay and a cost (the sticky pool with reacquisition). We deliberately keep the conclusions drawn from this study qualitative and structural —that a perfect single-step optimizer is provably suboptimal, and that the learned policy stays close to the true optimum—rather than transferring the specific numerical gaps to benchmark scale. Finally, the reported numbers carry no sampling noise—each policy’s expected return is computed by exact enumerated policy evaluation—and are averaged over 40 held-out seeds across multiple regimes, including a stationary control in which the multi-step effect is, by construction, absent. 

A control: when the future does not matter, our approximation is essentially optimal. We first isolate the case in which the multi-step structure is absent. Under stationary demand the same memories are useful at every step, so retention decouples over time into a sequence of independent single-step problems. Multi-step matters: a perfect single-step optimizer provably fails. The picture changes com-pletely once demand shifts over time. Now a memory that looks useless today may be precisely what a later query needs, and once it has been dropped it can return only through the delayed, costly reacquisition channel. The optimal policy must therefore pre-load such memories before their de-mand rises—a decision that pays off only in the future and is therefore invisible to any per-step reward. The consequence is stark: the exact single-step optimizer (Myopic), which maximizes the immediate reward perfectly at every step, is still 31 .5% away from the optimum under shifting demand and 19 .3% away when reacquisition is costly. Because Myopic is the best possible one-step policy and not a weak baseline, this gap cannot be attributed to a poor heuristic—it is direct, quantitative evidence that retention does not decompose into independent per-step problems. The multi-step formulation of Section 3 is thus a necessity, not a modeling convenience. 

Learning to look ahead closes the gap—and does it best among deployable policies. What recovers this large gap is accounting for the future when scoring memories. Ranking memories by their long-run rather than immediate utility—exactly the inductive bias OSL-MR encodes through its demand-aware features and Mixed-Score prior—collapses the Myopic gap of 31 .5% /19 .3% down to roughly 6% –11% . Among all deployable policies, the learned OSL-MR scorer comes closest to the optimum in every coupled regime: 10 .1% under shifting demand and 5.7% under costly reacquisition, ahead of the hand-tuned Mixed-Score greedy ( 11 .3% /6.6% ) and on par with it when there is no temporal structure to exploit ( 2.0% ). Strikingly, OSL-MR matches—and slightly beats— explicit receding-horizon planning (Lookahead-4: 12 .3% /5.2% ) while using no planning and only online-observable features at inference; deeper lookahead narrows the gap only monotonically with depth and never overtakes the learned scorer. The lesson is that the value of the multi-step view is realized not through expensive planning but by learning which memories will matter later— precisely what OSL-MR is built to do and precisely what a single-step objective cannot capture. The study directly answers the two questions that motivated it. (i) Does the multi-step structure mat-ter? Decisively yes: even the exact single-step optimizer leaves a 19 –32% optimality gap once de-mand shifts, so retention does not decompose into independent per-step problems and the sequential formulation of Section 3 is a necessity rather than a modeling convenience. (ii) How near-optimal is the deployable learned policy? OSL-MR stays within about 2% of the optimum when the future is irrelevant and within 6–10% when it is not—the closest of all deployable policies, and on par with model-based planners despite using no planning. ## 5 CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION 

We have presented OSL-MR, a framework that treats memory retention in long-horizon language agents as a sequential decision problem under budget constraints, delayed feedback, and partial ob-servability. Its central contribution is the formulation: to our knowledge, this is the first work to cast memory retention as a constrained stochastic optimization problem accounting for evidence utility, reacquisition cost, and stale risk, under a strict separation between online-observable features and offline-available supervision. Because the problem is NP-hard (Proposition 1) and partially observ-able, exact optimization is intractable, so we design a learning-based approximation that pairs a lightweight Mixed-Score heuristic (cold-start baseline and inductive prior) with an evidence learner trained from natural user-agent logs. On small, exactly-solvable instances of the same problem, this horizon-aware approximation is near-optimal under stationary demand and closes most of the optimality gap left by exact single-step optimization once demand shifts, achieving this through long-run utility scoring rather than expensive planning. Empirically, the learned policy consistently outperforms strong heuristics (recency, Generative Agents, and Mixed-Score itself) on LoCoMo and LongMemEval, especially under tight budgets, with the Mixed-Score prior improving precision and modestly improving recall, and direct evidence supervision as the primary driver. The strict observ-ability separation ensures that all reported gains reflect generalization under realistic information constraints. Our framework complements recent advances in memory writing (Mem0), compression (MEM1), and retrieval-side optimization (MemRL): while those target other stages of the memory lifecy-cle, OSL-MR is the first to formalize and optimize retention under hard budgets and observability constraints, bridging constrained stochastic optimization and learning-based approximation. Future work includes automatically learning the Mixed-Score feature groups and weights, more principled 19 Preprint freshness estimation beyond type-based decay, and scaling to embodied or tool-augmented agents via more efficient or amortized decision strategies.
